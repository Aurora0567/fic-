TITLE: The Technical Default Spread

ABSTRACT: We study the quantitative impact of lender control rights on corporate investment, asset prices, and the aggregate economy. We build a general equilibrium model in which the breaching of a loan covenant (technical default) entails a switch in investment control rights from borrowers to lenders. Lenders optimally choose low-risk projects, thus mitigating borrowers’ risk-taking incentives and lowering the cost of equity. This mechanism generates strong macroeconomic effects and mitigates the financial accelerator. Consistent with our model, proximity to technical default in the data is associated with 4.12% lower returns and lower exposure to systematic risk.

Traditional macro-finance models with financially constrained firms (Bernanke and Gertler 1989;Bernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist 1999;Kiyotaki and Moore 1997) treat banks as passive bystanders without any involvement in their borrowers’ repayment policies. In these models, when a borrower is solvent, the bank recovers the value of the loan and obtains interest payments, and when the borrower defaults, the bank recovers what is left of the borrower’s assets. In reality, banks exert significant effort to safeguard loan repayments. Using data on syndicated lending,Roberts and Sufi (2009a) document that around 97% of loans to large U.S. corporations contain at least one covenant. Using data from the U.S. shared credit registry,Chodorow-Reich and Falato (2022)show that around 6.5% of U.S. loans enter technical default by breaching a covenant in any given quarter, and that around 30% of U.S. loans were in technical default in the run-up to and during the financial crisis.

When firms enter technical default, banks can freeze credit lines, call back loans, and charge higher rates and fees. More generally, technical default entails a switch in control rights from borrowers to lenders, whereby banks influence and directly intervene in corporate investment decisions (Chava and Roberts 2008;Nini, Smith, and Sufi 2009).1Lenders have radically different payoffs than borrowers, suggesting that control rights reallocation may affect on corporate investment, risk-taking, and asset prices. However, the macro-finance literature has so far been silent on the impact of such widespread lender control rights on firm-level and aggregate outcomes.

In this paper, we study the impact of lender control rights on the aggregate economy and on the cross-section of equity returns. We develop a dynamic general equilibrium model based on theBernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist (1999)financial accelerator model, where entrepreneurs borrow from lenders to finance production. Our infinite-horizon economy is populated by entrepreneurs, lenders, firms, and workers. Firms have unique access to productive capital and labor, and hire myopic entrepreneurs to make static investment and financing decisions. Entrepreneurs borrow resources from risk-neutral lenders and, outside of technical default, allocate resources between productive capital and a risk-free asset. Our main theoretical departure fromBernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist (1999)is the inclusion of endogenous covenants in the loan contract.

We make two main assumptions on loan covenants. First, we assume that covenants are written on firms’ profits. This assumption is consistent with the vast prevalence of cash-flow-based (as opposed to stock-based) covenants in U.S. corporate lending (see, e.g.,Lian and Ma 2021).2Second, covenant violations trigger a switch of investment control rights to lenders, and lenders in control allocate the borrower’s assets by maximizingtheir ownpayoffs. This assumption is supported by extensive evidence on the active involvement of lenders in corporate policies when borrowers enter technical default (e.g.,Chava and Roberts 2008;Nini, Smith, and Sufi 2009;Nini, Smith, and Sufi 2012;Falato and Liang 2016).

In our model, the allocation of investment control rights happens after the loan contract is signed and before payoffs are realized. At the beginning of each period, entrepreneurs meet with lenders and negotiate a one-period contract specifying loan size, interest rate, and a covenant on asignalof the risky investment’s idiosyncratic profitability. If the idiosyncratic signal falls below the covenant threshold, investment control rights (ie, the right to choose between risky and risk-free assets) are assigned to the lender. If the idiosyncratic signal falls above the threshold, investment control rights are assigned to the entrepreneur.

In the middle of the period, the firm’s idiosyncratic profitability signal is realized and control rights are assigned to either the entrepreneur or the lender. The party in control makes investment decisions, and aggregate and idiosyncratic shocks are realized at the end of the period. If the value of the firm’s assets is higher than the loan value, the lender collects the loan principal plus interest, and the entrepreneur collects the residual value of the assets. If the value of the assets is lower than that of the loan, the firm is in default. In this case, the lender collects a fraction of the residual asset value, and the entrepreneur obtains nothing.

Our main theoretical result shows that, even if entrepreneurs are risk-averse, they always optimally choose full investment in the risky asset. This result highlights risk shifting incentives in our economy, whereby entrepreneurs protected by limited liability invest in a riskier manner than if they were fully financed by equity. On the other hand, lenders’ concave payoffs induce full investment in the risk-free asset conditional on technical default. As a result, our model predicts different investment risk based on the endogenous allocation of control rights: risky investment by the entrepreneur is associated with higher exposure to aggregate risk and expected stock returns than risk-free investment by the lender. This mechanism yields the empirically testable prediction that firms closer to and in technical default (and therefore more likely to experience a shift in control rights) should havelowerinvestment risk andloweraverage returns than firms further away from technical default.

We also prove that our general equilibrium model achieves aggregation, which allows us to study the macroeconomic implications of endogenous covenants and control rights transfers in a computationally tractable, representative agent framework. As in the data, with the help of endogenous covenants and procyclical variation in the average prospects of future profitability, our calibrated model generates countercyclical variation in the probability of technical default, a pattern rarely studied in the macroeconomic literature. Moreover, our model matches salient macroeconomic and asset pricing moments, such as the level and the volatility of the risk-free rate, the equity premium, and the volatility and autocorrelation of consumption, output, and investment growth.

Based on the calibration results, we show that the presence of covenants in loan contracts has strong mitigation effects on the financial accelerator. To do so, we compare the impulse response functions (IRFs) of key macroeconomic variables, such as output and investment in two calibrated economies: a benchmark economy where loan contracts include covenants, and an economy where loan contracts do not include covenants (close toBernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist 1999). We show that, in response to negative TFP shocks (and perfectly correlated positive risk shocks, as inChristiano, Motto, and Rostagno 2014), borrowers’ technical default probability increases. Lenders receive control rights (and make safe investment decisions) precisely in firms that receive bad profitability signals, which safeguards loan repayments. This relaxes lenders’ break-even conditions, and allows them to cut lending to “good” entrepreneurs not in technical default by less than if covenants were not present in the loan contract. As a result, following a negative TFP shock investment and capital prices decrease by 33.5% less in the benchmark economy than in the economy without loan covenants. In sum, lender control rights mitigate the financial accelerator mechanism—a vicious cycle induced by a decline in entrepreneurial net worth, a decline in the price of capital, and an increase in external finance premiums.

Next, we turn to the cross-section of firm investment and equity returns to test our mechanism. We use DealScan loan covenant data to construct a quarterly firm-level measure of distance-to-technical-default. As inMurfin (2012), strictness is the probability that a firm will breachanyof its covenant terms over the next quarter, and constitutes an ex ante measure of the likelihood of lender control rights assignment. Different from ex post measures of covenant violation, strictness is continuous, which allows us to study the relationship between distance to technical default and expected returns through standard portfolio sorting techniques. Using this measure, we study the empirical relationship between distance to technical default, investment risk, and stock returns.

Consistent with our model, we document a negative relationship between strictness and expected returns: a strategy that goes long on a portfolio of firms in the top quintile of the strictness distribution and short on a portfolio of firms in the bottom quintile earns an averagenegativeexcess return of 4.12% per year. The negative relationship between strictness and returns is driven by high-strictness firms (firms in the top quintile of the strictness distribution earn 7.7% lower excess returns than firms in the fourth quintile), and it survives a battery of cross-sectional asset pricing tests, robustness tests, and empirical specifications. The relationship between strictness and returns is not driven by financial health nor by distress (Campbell, Hilscher, and Szilagyi 2008;Bharath and Shumway 2008), and it also holds ex post for firms that indeed breach their covenants.

Consistent with our mechanism, we show that high-strictness firms have lower returns as a result of their lower investment risk. First, the strictness-return relationship is partly explained by differential exposure to theHou et al. (2021)expected investment growth factor: high-strictness firms have aggregate risk exposures similar to those of firms withlowexpected investment growth. Second, the growth of the U.S. gross domestic product (GDP) and that of Personal Consumption Expenditure (PCE) are priced risk factors in our data, and high-strictness firms have lower return exposure to these factors than low-strictness firms. Third, loan covenant strictness persistently reduces the sensitivity of investment, profitability, and cash flows to aggregate risk.

We interpret our empirical results as evidence of a shift in investment control rights when firms are close to technical default. Consistent with this mechanism, our model is able to generate a sizable technical default spread: high covenant strictness in the model is associated with low investment risk and low average returns. Taken together, our empirical results and calibration support lender control rights as a quantitatively important determinant of a firm’s investment, risk-taking, and cost of capital, both in the aggregate and in the cross-section.

Our paper contributes to three streams of literature. Building on the seminal contributions ofBernanke and Gertler (1989),Bernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist (1999), andKiyotaki and Moore (1997), an established stream of the macro-finance literature studies how financial frictions shape corporate investment decisions and the cost of equity.3

Recent work in this area highlights the pervasive presence of cash-flow-based covenants in lending contracts (Lian and Ma 2021), the trade-offs between covenants and interest rates (Green 2018), and the effects of covenants on investment policies and monetary policy pass-through (Greenwald 2019;Adler 2020;Xiang 2024).

Departing from most of this literature, in this paper we explicitly account for lenders’ different payoffs and investment incentives when they are in control of the firm, and we build on this insight to study the aggregate and cross-sectional implications of control rights allocation. The closest paper to ours in this field isGourio and Gete (2015), which studies the business cycle implications of endogenous loan covenants in theBernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist (1999)framework. While our baseline model builds onGourio and Gete (2015), its technical implementation and results substantially differ from those inGourio and Gete (2015). For example, we introduce a rich signal structure for the idiosyncratic profitability shocks, which allows us to generate heterogeneity in distance to technical default and to study the cross-sectional asset pricing implications of lender control rights.4To the best of our knowledge, our paper is the first to show that lender control rights can have quantitatively meaningful implications for business cycle dynamics in the time series and asset prices in the cross-section.

A long literature in corporate finance highlights loan covenants as a tool to mitigate agency problems (Jensen and Meckling 1976;Myers 1977;Smith and Warner 1979). Subsequent empirical studies show that covenant violations can affect firm investment (Chava and Roberts 2008;Nini, Smith, and Sufi 2009;Bradley and Roberts 2015) hiring policies (Benmelech, Bergman, and Seru 2021;Falato and Liang 2016), risk (Gilje 2016;Ersahin, Irani, and Le 2021), and value (Beneish and Press 1995;Harvey, Lins, and Roper 2004;Nini, Smith, and Sufi 2012). We complement this literature by showing that the allocation of control rights between borrowers and lenders has quantitatively important implications not only for individual firms but also for the aggregate economy. We also find that changes in risk-taking induced by technical default manifest themselves in the cost of equity capital, a result that we believe is new to this literature.

Our paper also contributes to the literature on production-based asset pricing (for a survey, seeKogan and Papanikolaou 2012), and to the broader literature linking investment to the cross-section of expected returns. In two closely related papers,Li, Wang, and Yu (2021)document that expected investment growth is positively related to expected returns in the cross-section, andHou et al. (2021)show that expected growth factor can significantly improve the explanatory power of theq-factor model. Different from these papers, we focus on the quantitative impact of lender control rights on expected stock returns. While our paper is also related to the asset pricing literature on financial distress, we show that control rights reallocation is a distinct economic mechanism compared to those previously proposed to explain the distress anomaly (Campbell, Hilscher, and Szilagyi 2008).5
We present a discrete-time, infinite-horizon general equilibrium model featuring a representative household of entrepreneurs and workers, a continuum of firms, and a national lender. Our model extends the workhorse financial accelerator model (Bernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist 1999) to include loan covenants and creditor control rights.

1.1 Model setup

Time is infinite and discrete. In each periodt, entrepreneurs sign one-period contracts with the national lender to make one-period investment decisions between a risk-free asset and risky capital. The lending contract specifies the size of the loan, its interest rate, anda covenant threshold. After the lending contract is signed, but before investment decisions are made, entrepreneurs and the lender observe a signal on the firm’s future idiosyncratic productivity. If this signal is above the covenant threshold, the entrepreneur retains control rights and decides how to allocate her own funds and the funds borrowed from the lender between the risk-free asset and risky capital. If the signal is below the threshold, investment control rights are assigned to the lender.

2 Quantitative Model Results: In this section, we first specify the exogenous aggregate shocks in our economy. We then calibrate the model and evaluate its ability to replicate key moments of macroeconomic quantities and asset prices at the aggregate level. Finally, we investigate the model’s aggregate quantitative implications. We show that the model generates a countercyclical variation in the aggregate technical default probability consistent with the data, and that allowing endogenous covenants in the optimal contract mitigates the financial accelerator mechanism. We also show that the model can produce a sizable technical default spread in equity returns.

2.1 Specification of aggregate shocks

We consider two aggregate shocks. First, we assume that the natural logarithm of aggregate productivity, ln ⁡ Z t ⁠ , follows the process ln ⁡ Z t − ln ⁡ ( Z ― ) = ρ z ( ln ⁡ Z t − 1 − ln ⁡ ( Z ― ) ) + σ z ϵ Z , t , (25) where Z ― is the steady-state level of Z t , and ϵ Z , t is white noise that follows an i.i.d. standard normal distribution. Second, we introduce shocks to the volatility of ω 0 and ω 1 (i.e, σ t 0 and σ t 1 ⁠ ), which we interpret as risk shocks following Christiano, Motto, and Rostagno (2014) . Specifically, we assume that the natural logarithms of σ t 0 and σ t 1 follow the process ln ⁡ σ t j − ln ⁡ ( σ ― j ) = ρ s ( ln ⁡ σ t − 1 j − ln ⁡ ( σ ― j ) ) + σ s j ϵ s , t , j = 0 , 1 , (26) where σ s 0 and σ s 1 are the standard deviations of the shocks to ln ⁡ σ t 0 and ln ⁡ σ t 1 ⁠ , respectively. 12

We further assume that the innovations terms, ϵ Z , t and ϵ s , t ⁠ , are perfectly negatively correlated, which implies the following structure for the innovation terms: [ ϵ Z , t ϵ s , t ] ∼ Normal ( [ 0 0 ] , [ 1 ρ Z , s ρ Z , s 1 ] ) . (27)

The perfect negative correlation between the innovation shocks indicates that a negative productivity shock is associated with a positive risk shock, consistent with increased uncertainty in bad times (see, e.g.,Jurado, Ludvigson, and Ng 2015;Bloom et al. 2018). These perfectly correlated shocks are a parsimonious way to generate positive correlation between contemporaneous consumption and investment.13

2.4 Cross-sectional moments: covenant strictness and equity returns

We also evaluate the model’s ability to replicate the cross-sectional return patterns that we observe in the data (see Section 3.2). To do so, we first simulate the model for 800 quarters, thus obtaining a time series of aggregate quantities and prices.

Using the aggregate simulated data, we draw idiosyncratic signals and shocks to their distribution and simulate a cross-section of 5,000 firms in each period. Finally, we obtain firms’ simulated returns in each period usingEquation (23).

Thanks to the aggregation properties of the model, the covenant threshold ω ― t 0 is the same across all entrepreneurs at the beginning of each period before the idiosyncratic shock ω i , t 0 is realized. As mentioned earlier, to make our model more realistic we assume that the exact realization of the idiosyncratic shock ω i , t 0 can be perfectly observed only by the entrepreneur and by the lender, but not by investors. Specifically, if the entrepreneur is not in technical default, investors can only observe the following noisy signal of ω i , t 0 ⁠ : η ^ i , t = ω i , t 0 + η i , t , (28) where η is an i . i . d white noise with a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1. If the firm enters technical default, investors can perfectly observe the idiosyncratic shock realization ω i , t 0 ⁠ . This assumption is consistent, for example, with the mandatory disclosure of material violations in publicly listed firms’ SEC filings.

Under these assumptions, we define strictness in the model as Strictness i , t M = { exp ( ω ― t + 1 0 − η ^ i , t + 1 ) exp ( max j ∈ I { ω ― t + 1 0 − η ^ j , t + 1 } ) if ω i , t + 1 0 ≥ ω ― t + 1 0 , 1 if ω i , t + 1 0 < ω ― t + 1 0 , (29) and we use this measure to conduct cross-sectional portfolio sorting using simulated data. When we normalize strictness in (29) by the largest value of ω ― t + 1 0 − η ^ i , t + 1 across all firms, we can confirm that strictness in the model is monotonically increasing in ω ― t + 1 0 − η ^ i , t + 1 ⁠ , and that its value is always lower than or equal to 1 when ω i , t + 1 0 ≥ ω ― t + 1 0 ⁠ .

We present the results of our cross-sectional replication inTable 3. In the model, high-strictness firms invest in less risky assets and earn a significantly lower average return than low-strictness firms. Quantitatively, the control rights allocation mechanism produces a 3% technical default spread consistent with the data.

2.5 Lender control rights and the financial accelerator

In this section, we present impulse response functions to aggregate shocks in our benchmark model and in a financial accelerator model without loan covenants (henceforth the “no-covenant model”) similar toBernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist (1999). This exercise allows us to demonstrate the role of endogenous covenants in mitigating the financial accelerator. We show that the model can generate countercyclical variation in the aggregate technical default probability, and that the endogenous variation of loan covenants and lender control rights over the business cycle mitigates the aggregate effects of the financial accelerator.

Figure 1plots the responses of quantities and prices to a one-standard-deviation negative productivity shock in our benchmark model (the dashed black line) and in the no-covenant model (the dotted black line).16This analysis produces five sets of results. First, in response to a negative productivity shock, capital prices and net worth-to-capital ratios decline as in standard macroeconomic models. Second, the entrepreneur’s net worth decreases by more than her debt after a negative TFP shock, such that the debt-to-net worth ratio (ie, leverage) increases. This in turn increases the cost of external finance and depresses investment, thus lowering entrepreneurial net worth in subsequent periods and generating a vicious cycle in economic activity. In sum, our model replicates the key mechanism of the financial accelerator. Third, because of the sharp decline in the return to capital (which negatively affects net worth), the entrepreneur’s actual default probability increases on impact and further increases in subsequent periods due to higher leverage and greater uncertainty. This leads to an increase in loan spreads (measured as the ratio of the loan rateRtB,Eto the risk-free rateRtD⁠).

Fourth, the entrepreneur’s technical default probability (ie,F(ω―t0|σt0)⁠) is countercyclical in our model, consistent with the aggregate data (as we show below). On the one hand, since TFP and risk shocks are negatively correlated, a higherσ0implies worse average signalsω0in bad states, which increases the probability of technical default. On the other hand, we find that in our model the optimal covenant threshold slightlydecreasesin bad times, which slightly reduces the probability of technical default.17Intuitively, decreasing covenant thresholds and maintaining control rights is particularly valuable for the entrepreneur in bad times, because it increases the states of the world in which she can invest in risky capital with high expected returns instead of giving up control rights to the bank. In all of our calibrations, the risk effect dominates, and the entrepreneur’s overall technical default probability increases in bad times, as shown in the bottom-left panel ofFigure 1.18On the credit supply side, a higher technical default probability relaxes the lender’s break-even condition, and allows the lender to offer slightly looser covenants, sightly higher interest rates, and higher leverage.

Fifth, a comparison between the benchmark model and the no-covenant model highlights significant mitigation effects for the financial accelerator. For example,Figure 1shows that investment rates and capital prices respectively decline by 4.69% and 0.13% on impact in the benchmark model, and by 7.05% and 0.20% in the model without covenants (implying a 33.5% lower decline in investment and capital prices when the contract includes covenants). This mitigation effect is driven by the countercyclical technical default probability generated by our model: In bad times, a higher technical default probability gives the lender safer payoffs, relaxes its break-even condition, and allows it to cut lendingto “good” entrepreneurs(ie, to entrepreneurs not in technical default) by less, thus mitigating the effects of negative TFP shocks on investment demand, capital prices, and net worth. Given this mitigation mechanism, we also observe relatively lower increases in loan spreads, external finance premiums, and actual default probabilities in the benchmark model than in the no-covenant model.

The mitigation effects documented inFigure 1can also be shown by comparing the simulated moments of the benchmark model with those of the no-covenant model.Table 2shows that, relative to the no-covenant model, the benchmark model features lower volatility in key macroeconomic variables, such as output, consumption, and investment, as well as a lower actual default probability. Overall,Figure 1andTable 2provide strong evidence of mitigation effects arising from time-varying changes in lender control rights. Consistent with previous work (Green 2018),Table 2also shows that the average loan rate in the benchmark model is 3.05% lower than the average loan rate in the no-covenant model. In other words, our calibration implies significant interest rate savings from the inclusion of covenants in loan contracts. Since covenants allow entrepreneurs to increase their leverage, this estimate can be interpreted as a lower bound on the interest savings from covenant inclusion in loan contracts.

2.6 Risk shocks and the financial accelerator

We conclude the quantitative section by highlighting the two roles of risk shocks in our model. First, risk shocks generate large variation in entrepreneurial net worth, and thus business cycle fluctuations that would otherwise be quantitatively small in a financial accelerator model with TFP shocks alone (see, e.g.,Christiano, Motto, and Rostagno 2014).19Second, risk shocks generate countercyclical technical default probabilities even if covenant thresholds are mildly procyclical. Overall, risk shocks are essential to generate both meaningful business cycle variation and mitigation effects for the financial accelerator in our economy.

InFigure 2, we present the results of this analysis. The procedure we follow to construct this figure is identical to the procedure used inFigure 1, with the only difference that we shut down risk shocks by fixingσt0andσt1at their steady-state levels. First, we find that in an economy without risk shocks, negative TFP shocks have a small effect on net worth, investment, and capital prices. As highlighted by the previous literature, TFP shocks alone do not generate large fluctuations. Second, absent risk shocks the technical default probabilitydecreasesin response to negative TFP shocks. This is because the covenant thresholdω―0decreases in bad times (consistent with our main model), while the idiosyncratic signalω0does not change. Lower technical default probabilities in bad times make lenders’ expected payoffs relatively riskier, tighten their break-even conditions, and induce them to cut lending to “good” entrepreneurs by more. As a result, the mechanism discussed in Section 2.5 works in the opposite direction, and the benchmark model features weakamplificationeffects relative to the no-covenant model.

3 Empirical Analysis and Cross-Sectional Quantitative Results: 3.1 Data

We use data from three main sources. Loan covenant data come from the Loan Pricing Corporation (LPC) DealScan database. This data set provides extensive coverage on syndicated and bilateral private loans made by bank and nonbank lenders to U.S. borrowers since 1984 (see, e.g.,Carey and Hrycray 1999;Bradley and Roberts 2015). The information contained in DealScan is sourced from listed firms’ SEC filings or directly obtained from borrowers and lenders, and it includes pricing, amount, and maturity of individual loans (also known asfacilities), as well as covenant terms for groups of loans included in the same lending contract (also known aspackages). Since DealScan is sparsely populated before 1996, and since portfolio sorting requires a sufficiently high number of observations in each portfolio, we drop pre-1996 loan observations from the data.

We use DealScan covenant data to construct a quarterly firm-level measure of distance to technical default. To build this measure, we focus on the five most common covenant ratios found on DealScan, namely, maximum debt to EBITDA, minimum interest coverage (EBITDA to interest expense), minimum fixed charge coverage (EBITDA to fixed charges, the sum of interest expense, debt in current liabilities, and rent expense), maximum leverage (long-term and current debt to total assets), and minimum current ratio (current assets to current liabilities). Collectively, these five ratios represent around 80% of all the DealScan covenant ratios.20

Our second main data source is Compustat. We use this data set and the updated link table ofChava and Roberts (2008)to construct a firm-quarter panel with the most restrictive threshold for each covenant type, as well as the annualized financial ratio associated with the covenant (Demerjian and Owens 2016). As detailed inInternet Appendix B.1, we then use these financial ratios and covenant thresholds to measure distance to technical default (strictness, as inMurfin 2012) at the firm-quarter level. Finally, we use CRSP data to obtain monthly stock returns for all the firms in our sample. Following standard practice, we drop firms in the financial and utilities industries, and we adjust for delistings as inFama and French (1993).21Our final sample starts with the first quarter of 1996, and it ends with the last quarter of 2016.

Figure 3shows the time-series properties of loan covenant strictness. Aggregate strictness is countercyclical, and it shows a general decreasing trend since the early 2000’s (as documented byGriffin, Nini, and Smith 2018). In theInternet Appendix, we show that strictness positively predicts future covenant violations.22Since covenant violations lead to the transfer of control rights to the lender (e.g.,Chava and Roberts 2008;Nini, Smith, and Sufi 2009), strictness is therefore an ex ante measure of lender control rights allocation.

3.2 Lender control rights and expected stock returns in the cross-section

3.2.1 Portfolio sorting

In our first exercise, we form five strictness-sorted portfolios in every quarter, and we compare the monthly excess returns across these portfolios in subsequent quarters. As inFama and French (1992), we allow a two-quarter lag for information to be incorporated into stock returns.Table 5shows the characteristics of the strictness-sorted portfolios in our sample. High-strictness firms have on average higher failure probability, EDF, past asset growth, and book-to-market than low-strictness firms, and lower profitability, dividend payouts, and credit ratings than low-strictness firms. There is no evidence of a relationship between strictness and investment-to-capital ratios, and evidence of a weakly positive relationship between strictness and financial constraints. High-strictness firms havehigher leveragethan low-strictness firms, suggesting that reductions in leverage when firms enter technical default (e.g., because of loan callbacks by lenders) are unlikely driving our cross-sectional findings on corporate risk-taking and equity returns.

Table 5also confirms the forward-looking nature of the sorting variable by showing a positive relationship between strictness and the probability of future technical default. Firms in the high-strictness portfolio are 4.5 times more likely to disclose a material covenant violation in thenext quarterthan firms in the low-strictness portfolio, and 1.75 times more likely to disclose a violation in the next quarter than firms in portfolio 4.23

Unconditionally, firms in the high-strictness portfolio earn 40% of the average excess returns of firms in the low-strictness portfolio. The average excess return of high-strictness firms is 2.64%, while that of low-strictness firms is 6.76%. Additionally, the relationship between current strictness and future excess returns is nonmonotonic: firms in the high-strictness portfolio earn only 25% of the excess returns earned by firms in portfolio 4. In other words, the negative relationship between strictness and returns is driven by firms in the highest strictness quintile.24However, as we show below, the relationship is not entirely driven by firms that are currently in technical default, suggesting that lenders may push even firms very close to (but outside of) technical default to change their investment risk.

We present three sets of results. First, the High-Low portfolio earns a marginally significant negative excess return of 4.12% per year. The results ofTables 6andInternet Appendix Table B2are consistent with our theory, which predicts that the technical default spread should arise from different exposure to aggregate risk as opposed to differences in return levels not spanned by risk factors. Our results suggest that the technical default spread can largely be attributed to differential exposure to theHou et al. 3.2.3 Lender control rights and firms’ financial health

Covenant thresholds can be different for different firms, and two firms may have the same financial health but different levels of strictness if their covenant thresholds are different. When two firms have similar covenant thresholds, however, strictness capturesbothfinancial health and distance to technical default. This raises the question of whether our results are due to time-varying financial conditions as opposed to lender control rights. We address this concern in two ways. First, inTable B4we augment the Fama-MacBeth specifications to control for the ratios underlying strictness, as well as for the interaction between strictness and these ratios. The baseline coefficient estimates inTable B4are economically and statistically similar to those inTable 7, and we do not find strong interaction effects with the underlying ratios, suggesting that our results are unlikely driven by time-varying financial conditions. Second, inTable B5we focus on a specific financial condition, financial distress. Since the financial distress puzzle is driven by firms with high default probability (see, e.g.,Garlappi and Yan 2011), in these tests we remove from the sample firms above the90thpercentile of the EDF distribution (columns 1 and 2) and those above the90thpercentile of the failure probability distribution (columns 3 and 4). We find statistically and economically similar results when we restrict the sample to nondistressed firms.

3.2.5 Additional robustness

In theInternet Appendix, we perform additional robustness tests on the results of the previous sections. First, in Tables B8-B10 we show that the results ofTable 6are robust to alternative specifications for the processes governing the (log) growth of the financial ratios underlying strictness, as well as to an alternative procedure to handle stock delistings. Second, inTable B11we show that our results are not driven by firms in technical default for multiple quarters, further reducing concerns that our results may be driven by subsets of firms with other persistent characteristics, and supporting the theoretical intuition that firmsjust enteringtechnical default should experience a decrease in their subsequent investment risk and returns.

Third, inTable B12we only include quarters in which firms issue new loans in DealScan. Although there are only about 25 firms on average in each portfolio, the portfolio level return pattern inTable B12are similar to our main results inTable 6, further mitigating concerns that our results are due to transitory financial conditions rather than to loan contracting. Fourth, inTable B13we include the WW Index and the SA Index (measured at the annual level) to the Fama-MacBeth specifications ofTable 7to confirm that our results are not driven by financial constraints. Fifth, in Tables B14 and B15 we repeat our main cross-sectional tests using pooled OLS regressions instead of Fama-MacBeth regressions. The results are statistically and economically similar to their Fama-MacBeth counterparts, and provide additional support for a nonlinear relationship between strictness and expected returns.

Sixth, inTable B16we show that the Fama-MacBeth results persist for up to 2 years, reducing concerns of confounding variation from covenant breaches’ announcements. Seventh, inTable B17we show that the cross-sectional results are economically and statistically similar when we use firms’ annual implied cost of capital (Hou, Van Dijk, and Zhang 2012) as an alternative measure of expected returns.26Eighth, inTable B18we show that the negative relationship between strictness and expected returns persists when we augment the Fama-MacBeth regressions with theHou et al. (2021)predictors of expected investment growth (ie, operating cash flows, changes in ROE, and the natural logarithm of Tobin’sq), reducing concerns that the EG factor exposures inTable 6may be due to differences in these predictors across high- and low-strictness firms. In other words,Table B18confirms strictness as a source of variation in future returns orthogonal to the predictors of investment growth.

Ninth, in Tables B19 and B20 we empirically examine the effects of covenant waivers using covenant waiver and amendment fee data fromBird et al. (2022).27InTable B19, we show that the main portfolio sorting results are virtually identical when we drop quarters in which a firm discloses paying material waiver fees, suggesting that waivers are unlikely driving our results. InTable B20, we also show that the payment of waiver fees slightly dampens the effect of a covenant violation on future returns. We interpret this result as suggestive, small-sample evidence that firms face a trade-off between paying loan renegotiation costs and giving up control rights.

3.3 Additional evidence on the mechanism

In this section, we present additional empirical results to support the predictions of our model. First, we show that strictness-sorted portfolios have different exposures to priced sources of aggregate risk. Second, in panel regressions we show that lender control rights affect firms’ investment and cash flow sensitivity to aggregate shocks. Third, using firm-level panel regressions we confirm the mild procyclicality of covenant thresholds suggested by our model.

3.3.3 Procyclical covenant thresholds

In Internet Appendix Table 12 , we present the results of quarterly panel regressions that provide evidence of procyclical variation in covenant thresholds consistent with our theoretical predictions. We first build a quarterly unbalanced panel at the borrower-lender-package-covenant type level containing, for each newly issued loan package on DealScan, information about the borrower, the lender, and the loan. Importantly, this panel contains threshold values for each of the five covenant types included in strictness. To remove threshold variation correlated with contemporaneous firm performance orthogonal to loan contracting, our specifications include the firm-level accounting ratios underlying the thresholds. To remove threshold variation correlated with time-varying lender characteristics (e.g., credit supply), our specifications also control for the lender’s Tier 1 ratio and leverage (total liabilities to total assets). In column 1 ofTable 12, we regress covenant thresholds on ΔGDP without any controls but firm and lead arranger fixed effects. For example, column 1 shows that a 1% decrease in lagged ΔGDP is associated a 0.495% decrease in the average covenant threshold value relative to the industry mean. The magnitude and statistical significance of our estimates are similar when we gradually include firm-level and loan-level covariates (thus controlling for orthogonal business cycle variation in borrowers’ health and loan contract terms), and when we replace ΔGDP with ΔPCE as the main independent variable. We also find that the estimated magnitudes are only slightly different when we include time-varying controls capturing the lead arranger’s financial health. This result confirms that lenders’ incentives may affect covenant levels (Payne 2018;Chodorow-Reich and Falato 2022), but also suggests that these incentives have a quantitatively small impact on our estimates.

4 Conclusion: In this paper, we build a dynamic general equilibrium model in which endogenous loan covenants allocate investment control rights between borrowers and creditors, and we study its quantitative implications for the aggregate economy and for asset prices in the cross-section. In our model, when borrowers’ expected cash flows fall below the contract-specified covenant threshold, lenders take control and optimally invest in low-risk projects to safeguard loan repayment. Such lender control rights make banks’ payoffs safer ex ante, and allow them to cut credit by less in bad times, thus mitigating the adverse effects of the financial accelerator. Additionally, low-risk investment by lenders in control reduces borrowers’ risk-shifting incentives and the cost of equity capital.

A calibrated version of our model matches the countercyclicality of aggregate technical default, the aggregate moments of key macroeconomic and asset pricing variables, as well as the technical default spread that we observe in the cross-section of equity returns. Empirically, firms closer to breaching a covenant have investment, profitability, and cash flows that are less exposed to aggregate risk, and earn 4.12% lower returns than firms with less binding covenants. To the best of our knowledge, our paper is among the first to show that lenders’ incentives can have large effects on borrowers’ cost of equity capital. We believe that incorporating the incentives of lenders and other stakeholders into traditional investment-based asset pricing frameworks can therefore constitute a fruitful avenue of future research.
