TITLE:The SOE Premium and Government Support in China's Credit Market
Abstract:  Studying China's credit market using a structural default model that integrates credit risk, liquidity, and bailout, we document improved price discovery and a deepening divide between state-owned enterprises (SOEs) and non-SOEs. Amidst liquidity deterioration, the presence of government bailout helps alleviate the heightened liquidity-driven default, making SOE bonds more valuable and widening the SOE premium. Meanwhile, the increased importance of government support makes SOEs more sensitive to bailout, while the heightened default risk increases non-SOEs' sensitivity to credit quality. Examining the real impact, we find severe performance deteriorations of non-SOEs relative to SOEs, reversing the long-standing trend of non-SOEs outperforming SOEs.
introduction:   The coexistence of state-owned enterprises(SOEs) with firms that are not state-owned (non-SOEs) creates the single most important divide in China's economy, with non-SOE firms competing with the less efficient but more privileged SOEs for economic, social, and political resources. On credit allocation, SOEs in China are known to have preferential access, although the actual magnitudes have not been well documented in the literature owing to the opacity of bank loans, the dominant credit channel in China.1Lacking this critical information, discussion of the real impact of the credit misallocation and the ensuing welfare losses is incomplete. Moreover, changing government policies can influence, either intentionally or inadvertently, the relative credit allocation between SOEs and non-SOEs, further complicating this enormously important topic.2
  In this paper, we study credit allocation between SOEs and non-SOEs using pricing information from China's credit market and provide the first comprehensive evidence in the literature on this important topic. Bypassing bank loans and shadow banking, we focus on the credit market, which, totaling $4.5 trillion by 2020, is second only to the United States. Unlike bank loans, the market prices of corporate bonds are driven exclusively by investors' concerns over the risk-and-return trade-off and therefore can best reflect firm-level credit conditions. We quantify the extent of SOEs' advantage in credit allocation using the SOE premium, measured as the difference in credit spreads between non-SOE and SOE bonds, controlling for credit ratings and other bond and issuer characteristics. Because of the fluidity of credit dispensation, the information captured by the SOE premium from the credit market is a reflection of the relative credit allocation not only in the credit market but also across all other credit channels in China.
  Our measure of the SOE premium reflects a deepening divide between SOEs and non-SOEs. Prior to 2018, the SOE premium for publicly listed firms in China fluctuates around a stable level of 20 basis points (bps), indicating that SOEs enjoy a moderate but nontrivial premium due to their perceived government support. After the release of the April 2018 New Regulations, we find that non-SOEs face a rapidly worsening credit condition relative to their SOE counterparts. Over just one quarter, the SOE premium exploded from 28 bps to an unprecedented 98 bps in 2018Q2. Recognizing the adverse effects on the private sector, starting in November 2018, the Chinese government offered reassurances and designed policies to support the private sector, but the SOE premium, or the non-SOE discount, deteriorated further, peaking at 154 bps in 2019Q3. It subsequently decreased to 93 bps as of 2020Q2.
  To understand the time-varying nature of the SOE premium, we build a structural default model that integrates credit risk, liquidity, and bailout. Throughout the model, the strength of the government support remains stable, and yet its importance and value increase sharply, resulting in an explosive SOE premium. Central to this result is the interaction between bailout probability and liquidity-driven default. In He and Xiong (2012), liquidity-driven default emerges from the interaction between liquidity and credit risk—increased debt-market illiquidity forces firms to incur higher losses in rolling over their maturing debt, leading equity holders to default at a higher fundamental thresholdVB$V_B$. Just as in He and Xiong (2012), where the interaction between liquidity and credit risk highlights the role of short-term debt in exacerbating rollover risk, in our model, the interaction between rollover risk and the potential of a government bailout highlights the role of SOE bonds in alleviating liquidity-driven default. Conceptually, our model offers the important insight that, during market-wide liquidity deterioration, the presence of a government bailout helps alleviate heightened liquidity-driven default for SOEs, while the lack of government support leaves non-SOEs exposed, driving a wedge between the two.
  Applying this important insight of our model to the liquidity crisis triggered by the April 2018 New Regulations, our model's implication for the explosive SOE premium is immediate. The primary purpose of the 2018 New Regulations is to contain the rapid increase of shadow banking and reduce systematic risk in the asset management industry. Over the short run, however, the unprecedented scope of the regulations severely shrinks the financing and refinancing channels of corporate issuers and weakens the demand for corporate bonds from asset managers, triggering a liquidity crisis in China's credit market. Consistent with the conditions laid out in He and Xiong (2012), we observe liquidity deterioration, reduced access to the bond market, and increasing defaults. Importantly, these effects are much more pronounced for non-SOEs than SOEs, reflecting the increased value of government support during the liquidity crisis. In essence, this classic episode of flight-to-safety has a Chinese characteristic—amid heightened concern over default risk, investors seek safety in SOE bonds and shun non-SOE bonds, amplifying the SOE premium.
  Empirical Tests on Credit Pricing. We test the credit-pricing implications of our model using the model-implied default measure (DM), which integrates empirical information on credit quality, liquidity condition, and extent of government support into one unified measure. Under our model,lnVt/VB$\ln \left(V_t/V_B\right)$is the distance between a firm's current asset valueVt$V_t$and its default boundaryVB$V_B$. When divided by the firm's asset volatilityσA$\sigma _A$, this distance becomes the standardized distance-to-default (DD), a concept proposed by Merton (1974) and popularized by Moody's KMV. In our empirical test, we use the DM, defined as the inverse of the DD. Following the approach of Moody's KMV, we first estimate issuer-level equity value and volatility, and then use them to back out, through our model's pricing relations, the firm's asset value and volatility. The final output is the quarterly firm-level DM. Intuitively, firms with a higher DM are closer to their default boundaryVB$V_B$and more likely to default. Within our model, the underlying driver of a firm's DM can be multifaceted. While Merton's model-implied DM is driven purely by the firm's fundamental credit quality, our DM unifies credit quality, government support, and liquidity.3Moreover, our DM highlights the interactions of these three components in explaining credit pricing, contrary to commonly used linear combinations of these components. Empirically, we find that while Merton's DM indicates that non-SOEs are on average healthier than SOEs, our DM suggests otherwise. Behind the opposing predictions is the presence of bailout probability in our model, which lowers the default boundary for SOEs and makes them safer than non-SOEs. Interacting bailout with illiquidity, this relative safety between SOEs and non-SOEs would further increase. Indeed, the quarterly difference in DM between non-SOEs and SOEs widens considerably after 2018Q2.
  Equipped with our model-implied DM, we first study the extent to which our model can explain the time-varying SOE premium. From 2014Q1 to 2018Q1, the SOE premium is on average 27 bps (t-statistic = 4.28) and then elevates rapidly to an average level of 113 bps (t-statistic = 7.76) from 2018Q2 to 2020Q2. Focusing first on the period in which the SOE premium is the most severe, we find that adding our DM as an explanatory variable reduces the SOE premium from the staggering 113 to 6 bps (t-statistic = 0.38), while adding Merton's DM further exacerbates the divide and increases the SOE premium from 113 to 116 bps.4Moreover, our unified DM also works during the relatively calm period from 2014Q1 to 2018Q1. Adding the DM reduces the SOE premium from the original 27 to 6 bps (t-statistic = 0.82), while adding Merton's DM increases the SOE premium from 27 to 32 bps (t-statistic = 5.05).
  Equipped with our model-implied DM, we also study the evolving nature of price discovery in China's credit market. While the information content of credit spreads has been studied extensively for the U.S. market, our paper offers the first comprehensive study of the Chinese market. From 2010 to 2020, this market is informed by two important shocks. The first-ever default in 2014Q1 changed investors' perception of credit risk and the 2018Q2 liquidity crisis underscored the increasing importance of government support. Using our model-implied DM to study price discovery in China's credit market, we find a market of improved price efficiency as investors price two emerging risk factors—default risk after 2014Q1 and the extent of government support after 2018Q2—into the credit market.
  Prior to 2014, as default had never occurred in China's credit market, the prevalent belief was that bonds are meant to be paid in full. Accordingly, prior to 2014Q1, DMs are unimportant in explaining credit spreads above and beyond credit ratings. Post-2014Q1, however, DMs emerge as an important pricing factor for both SOE and non-SOE bonds. From 2014Q1 to 2018Q1, a one-standard-deviation increase in Merton's DM is associated with an increase in credit spread of 24 bps for non-SOE bonds and 23 bps for SOE bonds. Introducing our unified DM, the extent of the price discovery improves moderately, indicating that this postdefault period is dominated by information with respect to “fundamental” credit quality, captured reasonably well by Merton's DM.
  Post-2018Q2, SOEs and non-SOEs are divided not only in the level of credit pricing (i.e., the explosive SOE premium), but also in the content of price discovery. From 2018Q2 to 2020Q2, under heightened concern about default risk, credit spreads of non-SOEs become significantly more sensitive to fundamental credit quality as captured by Merton's DM. Specifically, the economic significance of Merton's DM increases from the previous 24 to 79 bps for the non-SOE sample, but decreases slightly from 23 to 20 bps for the SOE sample. Indeed, with government support to help alleviate the increased liquidity-driven default, the emerging concern within the SOE sample is not the firm's health but rather the extent of its government support. Accordingly, using our unified DM to explain the credit spreads of SOEs, the economic significance more than doubles from the previous 23 to 51 bps, which is to be contrasted with the lack of improvement of Merton's DM in explaining the SOE sample. Moreover, our unified DM also outperforms Merton's DM in explaining the credit spreads for the non-SOE sample, and the economic significance improves from the previous 24 to 105 bps, reflecting the value of our model's unified approach.
  Empirical Evidence on the Real Impact. The rapid widening of the SOE premium reflects the worsening credit condition faced by non-SOEs in the broader economy. Examining its real impact, we compare the relative performance between SOEs and non-SOEs before and after the 2018 credit tightening, and find a rapid performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative SOEs. Prior to 2018, consistent with the common perception that non-SOEs in China are more efficient than SOEs, the performance gap in quarterly return on asset (ROA) between non-SOEs and SOEs oscillates around an average level of 0.55% and remains significantly positive. Post-2018, driven by the severe performance deterioration of non-SOEs, this performance gap collapses precipitously and becomes indistinguishable from zero.
  We further explain the performance deterioration of non-SOEs using firm-level credit deterioration, measured as the change in the default measure,ΔDM$\mathrm{\Delta}\textit{DM}$, realized at the event quarter of 2018Q2. Measuring the postevent change in performance,ΔROA$\mathrm{\Delta}\textit{ROA}$, by the difference between the postevent quarterly ROA and its preevent average, we useΔDM$\mathrm{\Delta}\textit{DM}$to predictΔROA$\mathrm{\Delta}\textit{ROA}$and find that, within the non-SOE sample, credit deterioration in 2018Q2 indeed leads to subsequent performance deterioration. Associated with a one-standard-deviation increase inΔDM$\mathrm{\Delta}\textit{DM}$at 2018Q2, the subsequent performance deterioration averaged over the first two quarters after 2018Q2 is0.33%$0.33\%$. Importantly, this credit channel is present only for the non-SOE sample, as we do not find strong evidence of predictability for the SOE sample. Moreover, using Merton's DM instead of our unified DM, the predictability for the non-SOE sample also goes away, underscoring the importance of our model's mechanism in capturing the true extent of the cross-sectional credit deterioration.
  Taking advantage of the informativeness ofΔDM$\mathrm{\Delta}\textit{DM}$, we further sort all firms—SOEs and non-SOEs—by theirΔDM$\mathrm{\Delta}\textit{DM}$as of 2018Q2 into two groups. Firms in the high group face worsening credit conditions in 2018Q2, while those in the low group are less affected. Interestingly, we find significant performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs in both groups, with0.79%$0.79\%$for the high group and0.31%$0.31\%$for the low group. The fact that the less affected group, as measured by our unified DM, exhibits a significantly lower relative performance deterioration of0.31%$0.31\%$is consistent with our hypothesis that credit deterioration of non-SOEs contributes to their performance deterioration. But the fact that even the less affected non-SOEs also underperform relative to their SOE counterparts indicates that the disadvantage faced by non-SOEs goes beyond the credit channel.
  Relative to our main hypothesis, we consider two alternative explanations, which attribute the underperformance of non-SOEs to their own vulnerability that is unrelated to the lack of government support. First, the U.S.-China trade war, which begins in March 2018, affects the non-SOEs more severely than the SOEs, causing the performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs. Second, SOEs can better withstand the 2018 credit tightening because the government-mandated SOE deleveraging from 2016 to 2017 helps strengthen their balance sheet relative to non-SOEs. Using sorting variables motivated by these two alternative hypotheses, we do not find strong evidence that the cross-sectional variation along these dimensions can explain the postevent performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs, contrary to the predictive results obtained using our model-implied DM.
  Related Literature. Our paper is motivated by the literature on the SOE-related credit misallocation and its impact on China's growth (Brandt and Zhu (2000), Dollar and Wei (2007), Hsieh and Klenow (2009), Song, Storesletten, and Zilibotti (2011), and Lardy (2019)).5Relative to this literature, which thus far focuses on SOEs' preferential access to bank loans, our paper is the first to approach the problem using pricing information from the credit market. Unencumbered by the opaqueness associated with the distributions of bank loans, we are able to uncover with precision and speed the relative credit allocation between SOEs and non-SOEs in China. For policymakers in China, our empirical findings should be informative and alarming, as the explosive SOE premium documented in our paper could have a destabilizing effect on China's credit market, and the rapid performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs calls for a better balance between the two indispensable segments of the economy.6
  Our paper contributes to the asset pricing literature by providing the first comprehensive study on the information content of credit spreads in China. For the U.S. market, the link between credit spreads and credit quality has been well established (Collin-Dufresne, Goldstein, and Martin (2001), Campbell and Taksler (2003), Bao (2009), and Bao, Pan, and Wang (2011)). For the Chinese market, however, this topic has not yet been systematically studied, and our paper helps fill the gap. By incorporating bailout probability into the structural default model of He and Xiong (2012) and taking the unified model to the data, our paper is also the first comprehensive empirical study to use a structural model that unifies credit risk, liquidity, and bailout.7Unlike the commonly used reduced-form approach, which introduces liquidity or bailout as additional explanatory variables, outside of credit risk, we take a unified approach by capturing the interactions of these components using our model-implied DM.
  Our paper is also related to the literature that studies the asset pricing implications of the presence of government support. Focusing on the impact of Lehman's default, Berndt, Duffie, and Zhu (2024) document large post-Lehman reductions in market-implied probabilities of a government bailout and significant increases in U.S. banks' debt financing costs. Balasubramnian and Cyree (2011) examine the emergence of too-big-to-fail after the 1998 Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) bailout. Kelly, Lustig, and Nieuwerburgh (2016) document the extent of too-big-to-fail during the 2008 crisis using the pricing difference between put options on the financial sector and those on individual banks. Related to this literature, we document the emerging importance of government support in driving the SOE premium and make the important observation that the value of government support increases during a liquidity crisis, deepening the SOE premium.8
  Finally, our paper is part of the emerging literature on the Chinese credit market.9Studying the value of the implicit government guarantee, Jin, Wang, and Zhang (2023) focus on the first large SOE default in 2015, and Ang, Bai, and Zhou (2023) and Liu, Lyu, and Yu (2021) on China's Chengtou bonds. Chen, He, and Liu (2020) study the link between the growth of Chengtou bonds and the 2009 stimulus package in China.

I. China's Credit Market and the SOE Premium:   The emergence of China's credit market is a recent phenomenon. From 2008 through 2020, domestic debt securities issued by China's nonfinancial companies increased by $4.3 trillion, from a negligible level in 2008 to $4.5 trillion in 2020, second only to the United States. For the bank-dominated financial system in China, the presence of this market-based credit channel has opened a new channel of debt financing that is cheaper and more efficient than traditional bank loans. Indeed, for nonfinancial firms in China, the emergence of this onshore market has increased their average ratio of market-based debt to bank debt from 3.8% in 2008 to 21.6% in 2020. As China further opens up its financial system, this onshore credit market has the potential of becoming a key component of the global fixed-income market, offering prospective international investors exposure to the real China. If the rapid growth of China's economy has been the story of the past three decades, moving forward, the maturation of China's financial markets and their integration into the global markets could well be the story of the coming decade.
  In this paper, we focus our analysis on corporate bonds in China. Similar in structure to their counterparts in the United States, corporate bonds in China are of three types: medium-term notes (MTNs), which account for the largest fraction and are traded in the interbank market, corporate bonds, which account for the second largest fraction and are exchange-traded, and enterprise bonds, which account for only a small portion of our listed sample and are traded in both markets. Focusing on the divide between SOEs and non-SOEs, we further identify the bond issuer via the NSOE dummy, which equals one for non-SOEs and zero otherwise. Though our sample includes bonds issued by listed and unlisted firms, our empirical analyses focus mostly on publicly listed SOE and non-SOE firms, which are larger and more important to the economy and whose financial statements and equity market information help us measure their credit quality and potential government support. Because of space limitations, we provide a brief summary of our data below. Further details on our data can be found in theInternet Appendix.
  We obtain data on bond and equity pricing, as well as the issuer-level balance-sheet information from Wind, a Shanghai-based data service. For the purpose of studying credit pricing, we construct a sample of bonds issued by SOE and non-SOE firms with actively traded stocks and corporate bonds. Specifically, we include fixed-rate bonds issued by nonfinancial firms and exclude bonds with maturity less than one year, bonds whose issuer has less than 10 trading days in the equity market during a quarter or missing financial statements during a quarter, sinkable bonds, and defaulted bonds.11To prevent potential data errors or outliers from driving our results, we also take the conservative approach and winsorize credit spreads at lower 0.5% and upper 99.5% of the sample.
  Our sample period runs from January 1, 2010 through June 30, 2020. Prior to 2010, the credit market in China is not sufficiently developed to have an adequate number of non-SOE issuers for our empirical analysis. We further separate our sample period into three subperiods by two landmark events in China's credit market: the first-ever default on March 4, 2014, and the New Regulations on Asset Management released on April 27, 2018. Accordingly, Phase I, from 2010Q1 to 2013Q4, covers the predefault period; Phase II, from 2014Q1 to 2018Q1, captures the first wave of defaults, which occurred mostly among unlisted firms in industries suffering from overcapacity; and Phase III, from 2018Q2 to 2020Q2, covers the postregulation period, when the second and much more severe wave of defaults occurred, mostly among non-SOE issuers.
  Our bond-level data, summarized in the Panel A of TableI, consist of quarterly bond prices, bond characteristics, and bond trading information. For pricing data, we compute quarterly yield-to-maturity for each bond using its last transaction price of the quarter. Following the convention in the market, we use Chinese Development Bank (CDB) bonds as the reference curve and calculate the credit spread as the difference between the corporate bond yield and CDB yield of the same maturity. For credit ratings, we merge our sample with the rating data set of Wind, which updates changes in rating by the major rating agencies in China. We convert letter grades into numerical numbers by assigning 1 to AAA, 2 to AA+, 3 to AA, 4 to AA−, and so on. In China, AAA is the top grade, AA+ and AA are in the middle, and AA− is generally of low quality, very few bonds are below AA−. Compared with the non-SOE bonds, the SOE bonds generally have higher ratings, larger issuance size, longer maturity, and lower coupon rate, and they tend to be older.
  A nontrivial number of corporate bonds in China are issued with embedded optionality, with call and put provisions being the two most common features. Puttable bonds give investors the option to sell back the bond at its face value at specified dates before maturity, making the bond more expensive and lowering its yield, while callable bonds give the issuer the option. Upon exercising the puttable or callable option, the issuer can usually further modify the coupon rate within a preset range to make the bonds less or more attractive. As non-SOEs are more prone to issuing puttable bonds, the presence of this optionality would in theory decrease credit spreads for non-SOEs, lowering the estimated SOE premium and biasing against our findings. Our empirical estimation adjusts for this optionality by assigningPuttableandCallabledummies to the respective bonds.12
  Another unique feature of China's bond market is the equal importance of the interbank market and exchanges as trading venues, with the former populated by large institutional investors and the latter by small and medium-sized investors. Unlike the U.S. corporate bond market, which is dominated by over-the-counter trading, both the interbank market and exchanges claim significant market share in bond trading. We use the dummy variableExchto indicate whether the observed bond price is from exchange trading to control for potential differences in investor behavior and regulatory authorities between these two markets. Finally, we report bond trading variables to reflect the overall liquidity condition of the market. For example,TradingDayscounts the number of trading days per quarter. Similar to the U.S. market, corporate bonds are on average infrequently traded. As shown in TableI, the average number of trading days per quarter is 15 for listed non-SOEs and 10 for listed SOEs. Relative to non-SOEs, SOE bonds are less frequently traded but with higher turnover.
  Issuer-level equity information is summarized in Panel B of TableI. For each of the 365 non-SOEs and 401 SOEs in our sample, we report the total market value of its equity in logarithm underEquitySize. We further report three important inputs used to measure the credit quality of a firm. We use daily stock returns within each quarter to measure quarterly equity volatility. To calculate firm leverage, we collect information on the firm's short- and long-term liabilities and its book value of assets from quarterly financial statements, and then calculateLeverageas the ratio of the total current liabilities plus total noncurrent liabilities to total asset value. Quarterly asset growth is computed as the growth rate of the asset value averaged over the past three years. Compared with non-SOE firms, SOEs on average have larger equity size, lower equity volatility, higher leverage, and smaller asset growth than non-SOEs.

Before 2018Q2, the SOE premium for the listed sample is estimated at 20 bps (t-statistic = 2.97) and 27 bps (t-statistic = 4.28) in Phases I and II, respectively. Controlling for credit rating and other bond characteristics as well as firm size, the SOE issuers on average enjoy a premium of about 20 bps over their non-SOE counterparts. As one notch improvement in credit rating is associated with a reduction in credit spread of around 50 bps during this period, this difference in financing cost between non-SOEs and SOEs is economically significant. Compared with the listed sample, the SOE premium for the unlisted firms starts to increase during Phase II, after the first default in 2014, consistent with the fact that the first wave of defaults occurs mostly for unlisted firms.
  Post-2018Q2, the SOE premium explodes rather suddenly and the average SOE premium increases to 113 bps (t-statistic = 7.76) in Phase III, equivalent to the full-sample difference in credit spread between a top-notch AAA-rated bond and a speculative-grade AA-rated bond. Behind this dramatic explosion in SOE premium are the fast-deteriorating credit market conditions for non-SOE issuers. As shown in TableI, without any controls, the average credit spread for non-SOE issuers is 203 and 204 bps in Phases I and II, respectively, before jumping to 354 bps in Phase III. By contrast, the average credit spread for SOE issuers increases relatively modestly over the three phases, from 121 to 132 and 170 bps, respectively. Since November 2018, recognizing the adverse effects on the private sector, the Chinese government at various levels has offered reassurances and designed policies to support the private sector, but the SOE premium, or the non-SOE discount, deteriorated further, peaking at 154 bps in 2019Q3. It subsequently fell to below 100 bps, equal to 93 bps in 2020Q2.

The first-ever default in 2014 marks the beginning of an erosion of this strongly held belief.13The first wave of defaults occurs around 2016Q1 and mostly to unlisted issuers, with quarterly default amounts ranging from less than RMB 1 billion to 12.2 billion, which is small compared with the total size of the credit market of RMB 16.1 trillion in 2016. Thereafter, non-SOEs take most of the blunt.
  In China, nonfinancial firms have access to three interconnected debt financing channels: bank loans, credit market, and shadow banking. Relative to the opaque bank loans that dominate the financial system in China, the emergence of the credit market has opened a new channel of debt financing that is cheaper, more transparent, and more efficiently allocated. For the asset management industry in China, the growth of the credit market has also expanded its investment frontier by offering an entirely new asset class—between the lower-yielding and lower-risk government bonds and the higher-yielding and higher-risk equity market. Indeed, the growth of the onshore credit market is closely connected with demand from the fast-growing asset management industry in China.
  The government-led credit tightening started around 2017, driven by concerns over unsustainable debt burdens fueled by the rapid expansion of the shadow banking sector. Estimated by Moody's Quarterly China Shadow Banking Monitor, the size of shadow banking stood at RMB 64.5 trillion by end-2016, of which 30.1 trillion comprise assets funded by the Wealth Management Product (WMP) and 19.5 trillion by entrusted and trusted loans. Such shadow banking players are also dominant in the corporate bond market, connecting the shadow banking sector to the credit market.
  New Regulations on Asset Management were announced in April 2018 with the primary purpose of restricting the sharp increase in the shadow banking sector and reducing systematic risk in the long run. The new rules forced asset managers to adopt net asset valuation and abandon amortized accounting, banned the practice of principal and return guarantees to fund investors, limited the scope of maturity mismatch of assets and liabilities, forbid multitier nesting, fund pooling, and channel business, and narrowed the scope of qualified products and qualified investors. Although the target of the 2018 New Regulations was the asset management industry in the shadow banking sector, the dominance of such players in the credit market means that the liquidity shock to the shadow banking sector was transmitted to the credit market almost instantly.
  The 2018 New Regulations affect bond market liquidity through both direct and indirect channels. In particular, by banning asset management products from investing in nonstandard assets, the new regulations impact firms (particularly non-SOEs) indirectly through their inability to roll over their debt through the shadow banking sector. Moreover, the new rules significantly reduce the attractiveness of asset management products, limit regulation-affected asset managers' capacity to delay the realization of market risk, and force these asset managers to value safety over yield. As a result, the fractions of holdings by regulation-affected asset managers in both the MTNs market and corporate bonds market decrease.14Given the transparency of the credit market over the opaqueness of bank loans and shadow banking, the immediate liquidity impact of the 2018 government-led credit tightening was felt and observed most visibly in the credit market. In other words, although our empirical evidence focuses on the credit market, the interconnectedness of the three credit channels means that the government-led tightening brought about by the 2018 New Regulations affects the credit and liquidity conditions of the entire economy.
  To better understand the link between the liquidity shock triggered by the 2018 New Regulations and the post-2018Q2 explosion of the SOE premium, we build on the framework of He and Xiong (2012). A formal development of the model is to be presented in SectionII. Here, we map the key insight of their model to our setting and provide empirical evidence on corporate bond liquidity, issuance, and defaults surrounding the 2018 New Regulations. As laid out in He and Xiong (2012), liquidity deterioration, increased rollover risk, and higher default rates are important implications of their model. Below we provide related empirical evidence surrounding the 2018 New Regulations.
  First, with respect to liquidity, we use the price-based illiquidity measure proposed by Bao, Pan, and Wang (2011) and compute the monthly bond-level gamma, which equals the negative ofcovΔpt,Δpt−1${\text{cov}}\left(\Delta p_t, \Delta p_{t-1}\right)$, whereΔpt$\Delta p_t$is the dayt$t$change in the log of end-of-day transaction prices. To estimate the monthly gamma, we require the bond to have at least 10 pairs ofΔpt$\Delta p_{t}$andΔpt−1$\Delta p_{t-1}$during a month. This filters out a large fraction of the bonds. As such, our estimated gammas correspond to the most liquid bonds in our sample. To further compare SOEs and non-SOEs of similar ratings and maturities, we apply our calculations to a subsample with bond ratings between AA and AAA and maturity between two and five years. For this sample of bonds, we find that during Phase II, SOE and non-SOE bonds are of similar magnitude in gamma, with SOEs slightly less liquid than non-SOEs. Post-2018Q2, however, we observe a sharp increase in the illiquidity measure for both SOEs and non-SOEs, indicating an overall deterioration of liquidity conditions after the 2018 New Regulations. Moreover, the increase in gamma is more pronounced for non-SOEs relative to SOEs. As shown in Panel A of FigureIA.1of theInternet Appendix, from Phase II to III, the average gamma increases from 0.07 to 0.27 for non-SOEs, compared with an increase from 0.09 to 0.17 for non-SOEs, indicating that post-2018Q2 liquidity worsens by a factor of 3.8 for non-SOEs and 1.9 for SOEs. Given that we estimate gamma for the most liquid bonds in our sample, the situation for the less liquid bonds would be even more severe.
  As a less direct measure of liquidity, we use quarterly turnover as summarized in TableI. We find supportive evidence. For example, quarterly turnover for both non-SOEs and SOEs drops from Phase II to Phase III, but the reduction in trading activity is more pronounced for the non-SOE bonds. Specifically, average quarterly turnover is around 31% for both non-SOEs and SOEs in Phase II, and drops to 20% for non-SOEs and 27% for SOEs in Phase III. Overall, our evidence suggests that liquidity conditions deteriorate after the 2018 New Regulations and that such deterioration is more severe for non-SOEs than SOEs.
  Second, with respect to rollover risk, we compare the amount of new issuance by SOEs and non-SOEs in the credit market. We find that the fraction of new issuance by non-SOEs drops significantly after the 2018 New Regulations, reflecting non-SOEs' increased difficulty in issuing new bonds to roll over their existing debt. Focusing on the subsample of corporate bonds rated between AA and AAA, the fraction of new issuance by non-SOEs drops from 39.1% to 19.4%, when averaged over two years before and after 2018Q2. Panel B of FigureIA.1further shows the average difference in bond issuance size between non-SOEs and SOEs, which is estimated from the quarterly firm-level regression after controlling for size and industry. We can see a strong decreasing trend since the 2018 New Regulations. Meanwhile, the requirement on credit rating has also increased for new issuance. From Phase II to Phase III, the average numerical rating at issuance decreases from 2.4 to 1.6 for non-SOEs and from 1.7 to 1.3 for SOEs. As lower numerical ratings imply higher credit ratings, these changes indicate that some issuers have lost access to the corporate bond market in Phase III, with this situation more severe for non-SOEs. Overall, we see a pattern of reduced access to credit by non-SOEs in the corporate bond market after the 2018 New Regulations.
  Third, with respect to defaults, we observe unprecedented defaults by non-SOEs starting in 2018Q3. Listed non-SOE issuers, who remain largely intact during the first wave around 2016Q1, are severely hit and account for 37% of the total amount of defaults in the credit market in 2019Q4. Meanwhile, the total amount of defaults has also increased dramatically, from RMB 14.4 billion in 2018Q2 to over RMB 50 billion in 2018Q4. This continues to represent a small amount compared to the overall size of the credit market, but the fact that over 90% of defaults occur among non-SOE issuers is a clear signal that these are the more vulnerable issuers. We also compute the one-year default rates for our sample of bonds constructed for credit pricing. The default rate for listed non-SOEs increases rapidly from less than 5 bps before 2018 to 35 bps in 2018Q2, while the average default rate for listed SOEs remains close to zero at around 2 bps.
  Overall, the 2018 New Regulations have a profound effect on the corporate bond market. Consistent with the mechanism laid out in He and Xiong (2012), we observe worsening liquidity conditions, reduced access to the bond market, and increasing defaults. Important for our paper, these effects are much more pronounced for non-SOEs than SOEs, reflecting the increased value of government support during a liquidity crisis. Just as in He and Xiong (2012), where the interaction between liquidity and credit risk highlights the role of short-term debt in exacerbating the rollover risk, in our paper, the interaction between rollover risk and the potential of government bailout highlights the role of SOE bonds in alleviating liquidity-driven default.

II. The Model:   We work with a unified framework by incorporating government bailout into the structural model of He and Xiong (2012). Our setting directly follows that of He and Xiong (2012). For completeness, we provide a brief sketch of the model.
  Letd(Vt,m)$d(V_t,m)$be the market price of a newly issued bond, which is used to replace the maturing bond with principalp$p$. Depending on the liquidity condition in the secondary market,d(Vt,m)$d(V_t, m)$can be different fromp$p$. Whend(Vt,m)<p$d(V_t, m)&lt;p$, the firm incurs a rollover loss. He and Xiong (2012) model the secondary bond market liquidity condition via liquidity-shocked bond investors, each experiencing an idiosyncratic liquidity shock whose arrival time is governed by a Poisson process with intensityξ$\xi$. Upon the arrival of a liquidity shock, the investor pays fractionk$k$of the bond value as a cost.
  With the introduction of a government bailout in equation (6), we can differentiate SOEs from non-SOEs by considering two firms with different bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$. For non-SOEs, we assume thatπg$\pi _g$is always zero, reducing them to the setting studied in He and Xiong (2012). For SOEs, the bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$is always nonzero and the varying magnitude ofπg$\pi _g$can be used to model SOEs with varying degrees of government support. Following He and Xiong (2012), for a given value ofVB$V_{B}$, we can solve the bond value from equation (3) with boundary conditions (4) and (6).
  Consider two bonds with identical debt structures, except one is issued by an SOE with bailout probabilityπg>0$\pi _g&gt;0$and the other is issued by a non-SOE withπg=0$\pi _g=0$. The SOE premium is defined as the difference between their yields.

  Central to the key determinants of the SOE premium is the interaction between government bailout and liquidity-driven credit risk (i.e., rollover risk). As He and Xiong (2012) shows, over time, rollover risk increases with deteriorating liquidity condition (i.e., higherξ$\xi$); while across bonds, rollover risk increases for those with shorter maturity (i.e., lowerm$m$). Adding bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$to this setting, we introduce a new form of cross-issuer variation that is unique to China's credit market. Under our model, SOE issuers with positive bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$are safer, with lower default probability, than non-SOE issuers withπg=0$\pi _g=0$. The value of that safety can be measured directly from the corporate bond market via the SOE premium. As the liquidity condition deteriorates with increasingξ$\xi$, government bailout becomes more valuable in alleviating the heightened rollover risk, and the SOE premium increases accordingly.
  To illustrate numerically how the interaction between bailout probability and liquidity shock affects bond pricing and the SOE premium, we calibrate our model by adopting the calibration parameters from He and Xiong (2012), with minor adjustments to match our sample. We conduct a more comprehensive estimation of our model in SectionIII.
  For the general environment, we set risk-free rater=4%$r=4\%$and tax rateτ=25%$\tau =25\%$. At the firm level, we set bond recovery rate given defaultα=50%$\alpha =50\%$, payout rateδ=2%$\delta =2\%$, and asset volatilityσ=15%$\sigma =15\%$, estimated from TableI. For bond market liquidity, we set transaction cost parameterk=1.0%$k=1.0\%$and liquidity shock intensity parameterξ=1$\xi =1$. For debt structure, we set bond maturitym=1$m=1$, current firm fundamental valueV0=100$V_0=100$, annual coupon paymentC=2.59$C=2.59$, and average bond principalP=49.64$P = 49.64$. With this set of baseline parameters, the one-year newly issued par bonds have a credit spread of 150 bps under the He and Xiong (2012) model, equivalent to a non-SOE bond with bailout probabilityπg=0$\pi _g=0$.
  While the effect of liquidity-driven default is well documented in He and Xiong (2012), what is new in our model is the interaction between bailout and liquidity-driven default. Fixing the model at its baseline parameters, we vary the two key parameters of our model, namely, bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$and liquidity shock intensityξ$\xi$, and report the credit spread in Panel A and the SOE premium in Panel B of Figure2. Consistent with our intuition, credit spreads decrease in bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$and increase in liquidity shock intensityξ$\xi$. More importantly, the sensitivity of credit spread to bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$is mild for smallξ$\xi$, but increases rather dramatically withξ$\xi$. As shown in Panel A, withξ=2$\xi =2$, the convex relation between credit spread and bailout is especially prominent nearπg=0$\pi _g=0$. Mapping this observation to our setting, non-SOEs withπg=0$\pi _g=0$and SOEs with positiveπg$\pi _g$, we see that as the overall liquidity condition deteriorates withξ$\xi$moving toward two, the credit spreads of non-SOE and SOE bonds both increase. But the nonlinear interaction betweenξ$\xi$andπg$\pi _g$means that the credit spread of the non-SOE bond increases to a much more greater extent, giving rise to the explosive SOE premium.
  The same observation can be made in Panel A of TableIII, where we report the credit spreads for bonds issued by firms with varyingπg$\pi _g$. Focusing on the non-SOE bond withπg=0$\pi _g=0$and the SOE bond withπg=0.6$\pi _g=0.6$, we see that whenξ=1$\xi =1$, the credit spread is 150 bps for the non-SOE bond and 100 bps for the SOE bond. The corresponding SOE premium is 50 bps, as reported in Panel B of TableIII. Increasingξ$\xi$from one to two, the credit spread increases to 295 bps for the non-SOE bond and 201 bps for the SOE bond, resulting in an SOE premium of 94 bps atξ=2$\xi =2$, which almost doubles the SOE premium atξ=1$\xi =1$. It should be emphasized that in our current model calibration, as well as in the later empirical estimation, we use the same liquidity parameter for non-SOE and SOE bonds. As such, the impact of illiquidity does not enter directly into the SOE premium. Instead, the result of an increasing SOE premium with increasingξ$\xi$is driven by the interaction between bailout probability and liquidity-driven default risk, demonstrating the key mechanism of our model. Hit by the same liquidity shock, non-SOEs differ from SOEs in that, without a government bailout, they are more vulnerable to an increase in liquidity-driven default. Conversely, government support helps alleviate the rollover risk for SOEs, making them safer. In the extreme case in whichπg=1$\pi _g=1$, there will be no liquidity-driven default because the government will always bail out the firm no matter how largeξ$\xi$is. In this case, the sensitivity of credit spreads toξ$\xi$will depend only on the pure liquidity channel.
  The impact of the bailout probability on the liquidity-driven default is further shown in Panel C of TableIII, which reports the model-implied one-year default probability for varying bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$. In calculating the actual default rate, we set asset growth under the actual probability measure toμ=15%$\mu =15\%$following TableI. Demonstrating the liquidity-driven default of He and Xiong (2012), increasingξ$\xi$increases the one-year default rate for both SOEs and non-SOEs. Reflecting the interaction between the bailout and liquidity-driven default, we find that the one-year default rate of the non-SOE bond increases much more strongly with increasingξ$\xi$than that of the SOE bond.
  Panel D of TableIIIshows how the maturitym$m$affects the SOE premium. For brevity, we fix the bailout probability to 0.6 for the SOE bond and to zero for the non-SOE bond. Whenξ=1$\xi =1$, we can see that the SOE premium decreases as maturity increases, implying that the SOE premium is more prominent when rollover pressure is higher. More importantly, the difference in the SOE premium (or multiplier) between a high-ξ$\xi$case (ξ=2$\xi =2$) and a low-ξ$\xi$case (ξ=1$\xi =1$) also becomes larger when maturitym$m$decreases (i.e., rollover pressure increases). In other words, our results become more significant when the rollover risk is higher, consistent with the main intuition in He and Xiong (2012).
  Moving away from the region ofπg=0$\pi _g=0$, which differentiates non-SOEs from SOEs, our model has implications for credit pricing among SOEs withπg>0$\pi _g &gt;0$. As illustrated by Panel B of Figure2, under the sameξ$\xi$, the sensitivity of the SOE premium toπg$\pi _g$eventually tapers off whenπg$\pi _g$gets close to one. For lowξ$\xi$, that transition occurs close toπg=0$\pi _g=0$, indicating that the value of government support can be proxied by a zero-one variable, akin to our non-SOE dummy. In effect, whenξ$\xi$is low, the deciding factor is whether the firm has government support, but asξ$\xi$increases, that transition point moves further away fromπg=0$\pi _g=0$. For example, fixingξ=2$\xi =2$, the SOE premium increases rather rapidly within the range ofπg∈[0,0.4]$\pi _g \in [0, 0.4]$and then tapers off. This implies that asξ$\xi$increases, whether a firm has government support (i.e., the SOE dummy) is not sufficient to determine the magnitude of its SOE premium. Instead, the extent of government support (i.e., the actual magnitude ofπg$\pi _g$) becomes important in determining the SOE premium. This observation turns out to be important as we apply our model empirically to study the price discovery for SOE bonds, before and after the 2018 New Regulations.

III. Empirical Tests on Credit Pricing:   This section takes our model to the data by first constructing our model-implied DM, which unifies the three components of our model, namely, credit, liquidity, and government support, into a single measure. We then examine our model's implications for credit pricing, including explaining the SOE premium and studying the price discovery.
  Proposed by Merton (1974) and popularized by Moody's KMV, the concept of DD evaluates, in unit of standard deviation, the distance between a firm's current asset value and its default boundary. Guided by structural models of default, it is an effective and disciplined way to measure issuer-level credit quality. In our empirical study, we use the DM, which is the inverse of the DD. A firm with a higher default measure is closer to its default boundary and more likely to default.17
  At each quartert$t$and for each issueri$i$, the empirical construction ofDMt,i$_{t,i}$feeds the model firmi$i$’s balance sheet and equity information in quartert$t$, and then brings back the model-implied DM. We start our exposition with Merton's DM, and then move on to construct the DM implied by our model, incorporating the issuer-level government support (i.e.,πg$\pi _g$) and the economy-wide liquidity condition (i.e.,ξ$\xi$). We refer to it as a unified DM, as it combines information on credit, liquidity, and government support into a single measure.
  For each quarter, we use the average growth rate of the asset value over the past three years to estimateμ$\mu$, the default boundaryK$K$equals the firm's current liabilities plus one-half of its long-term liability, and the firm's equity value equals the firm's market capitalization, calculated by multiplying the quarter-end stock price by common equity shares outstanding. For the equity volatilityσE$\sigma _E$, we use daily equity returns within the quarter, requiring that the issuer has at least 10 trading days in the quarter. For the risk-free rate, we use the one-year bank deposit rate. With these inputs, we estimate the quarterly asset valueV$V$and asset volatilityσA$\sigma _A$from equation (11) and compute the quartert$t$DM by equation (10).
  Our DM is unified in that it combines information on credit, liquidity, and bailout into a single measure. The focal point of the measure is the endogenous default boundaryVB$V_B$, driven by the interaction of these three components. Relative to Merton's measure, the construction of the unified DM requires two additional pieces of information: bailout and liquidity. To capture the deteriorating liquidity condition triggered by the government-led credit tightening, we set the liquidity parameter toξ=1$\xi =1$before 2018Q2 andξ=2$\xi =2$after 2018Q2. As illustrated in equation (3),ξk$\xi \,k$exerts a liquidity cost on the pricing of the bond, and settingk=1.0%$k=1.0\%$andξ=1$\xi =1$translates to a liquidity cost of 100 bps per unit value of the bond, which is also consistent with the average bid-ask spread in the data estimated from the exchange-traded bonds. In our base-case estimations, we set the cross-issuerξ$\xi$andk$k$to be the same for both non-SOEs and SOEs, although we also consider an alternative version allowing for heterogeneous liquidity parameters.
  To calibrate the issuer-level bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$, the simplest approach is to setπg=0$\pi _g=0$for non-SOEs and a positiveπg$\pi _g$, say 80%, for SOEs. But as discussed in SectionII.B, with increasingξ$\xi$, a simple yes-no dummy is not sufficient to capture the sensitivity of the model-implied SOE premium toπg$\pi _g$. To account for the extent of government support above and beyond the non-SOE dummy, we use the issuer-level government holdings data. Constructed from the ground up, our government holdings variable measures the sum of equity holdings by all government-related entities within the top 10 shareholders for each publicly listed firm.19The exact mapping from government holdings to bailout probability is influenced by the observation that while more government holdings is associated with higher bailout probability, the relation is not necessarily linear. Small increases in government holdings can be translated to large increases in bailout probability when the level of government holdings is low, but above a certain threshold this effect tapers off. In our study, we use a polynomial function to capture this form of nonlinearity and keep the mapping fixed over time.20
  For other parameters in the model, we use the same value as in the model calibration detailed in SectionII.B, with the exception of debt principalP$P$and couponC$C$. We calibrate the total debt principalP$P$as the sum of long- and short-term liability for firmi$i$in quartert$t$. For the coupon rate ofc$c$per unit of principal, we set it to the average coupon rate for firmi$i$in quartert$t$.
  Summary statistics for the model-implied DM are reported in TableI, along with the respective estimates of asset value and volatility backed out from equity value and volatility via equation (13). Over the full sample, Merton's DM is on average 22.56% for SOEs and 21.19% for non-SOEs, indicating that non-SOEs are in general healthier than SOEs according to the Merton model. By contrast, as illustrated in Panels C and D of Figure3, our unified DM paints a different picture and assigns the non-SOEs as the more risky group by a wide margin. Behind this different message is the fact that our unified DM evaluates the credit quality of a firm by integrating the firm's “fundamental” credit quality as captured by Merton's DM together with the bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$and liquidity parameterξ$\xi$. A clean decomposition is difficult as it is the interaction of these three forces that matters for the evaluation of credit risk. Nevertheless, it is instructive to consider how the two key parameters (πg$\pi _g$andξ$\xi$) influence our unified DM.
  Government holdings, a key input to the empirical construction of our unified DM, is on average 52.0% for the SOE sample and exhibits quite large cross-issuer variation, as shown in Panel A of Figure3. For the non-SOE sample, government holdings is markedly lower—an average of 2.8% for privately owned enterprises (POEs) and 12.5% for other non-SOE firms, and the cross-issuer variation is fairly narrow. Moreover, as shown in Panel B of Figure3, the time-series variation of government holdings is stable during our sample period. In particular, the government holdings for SOEs, which is mapped into the issuer-level bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$, does not exhibit large increase during Phase III. At the same time, however, the unified DM increases significantly for non-SOEs relative to SOEs during Phase III, as shown in Panel D of Figure3. In other words, the strength of the bailout remains the same, while the value of that bailout increases during Phase III, consistent with our model's mechanism.
  The presence of the positive bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$for SOEs lowers the default boundary and reduces the DMs for SOEs relative to non-SOEs. This message is loud and clear in the empirically estimated DMs. Contrary to Merton's DM, our unified DM indicates that SOEs are significantly healthier than non-SOEs. As shown in TableI, the unified DM is on average 21.94% for SOEs and 33.43% for non-SOEs. As plotted in Panel D of Figure3, the difference in the unified DM between non-SOEs and SOEs is consistently positive. Moreover, interacting bailout probabilityπg$\pi _g$with the liquidity parameterξ$\xi$, our model further indicates an increased value of bailout probability during liquidity deterioration. Consistent with this view, in Panel D of Figure3, we see that the difference in the unified DM between non-SOEs and SOEs oscillates around an average level of 8.42% in Phase II and widens to 12.20% in Phase III, reflecting this interaction effect.
  To illustrate the role ofξ$\xi$in generating the difference in the unified DM between non-SOEs and SOEs, we can perform a hypothetical calculation by turning off the liquidity component. Holding the other parameters fixed, particularly the asset values and volatilities that have already been backed out under our specification ofξ$\xi$(i.e.,ξ=1$\xi =1$before 2018Q2 andξ=2$\xi =2$afterward), we compute the unified DM withξ=0.001$\xi =0.001$. The difference in DM between non-SOEs and SOEs decreases to an average level of 5.30% in Phase II and remains little changed at an average level of 5.53% in Phase III. Compared to 8.42% in Phase II and 12.20% in Phase III, this reduction in the unified DM follows directly from our model's key mechanism. Keeping all parameters fixed, including asset values and volatilities, a higherξ$\xi$intensifies liquidity-driven default. Under such a situation, the role of government support in alleviating liquidity-driven default becomes more important. Hence, we see a larger difference in the unified DM between non-SOEs and SOEs.
  Unlike the example above, where the amount of liquidity-driven default can be dialed up and down using the liquidity parameterξ$\xi$while holding firm fundamentals fixed, the actual empirical construction of the unified DM is constrained by the data, particularly the equity market information via equation (13). It is important to point out that, moving from Phase II to III, the further divergence in unified DM between non-SOEs and SOEs is not created mechanically by the increase inξ$\xi$. Rather, it is driven by equity market information. Specifically, in constructing the unified DM, we use market-observed equity value and volatility to first back out the unlevered asset value and volatility, and then use these values to compute the empirical DM. In both processes, the liquidity parameterξ$\xi$is involved, but with opposing effects. In the first step, a higherξ$\xi$would lower asset volatility and increase asset value, making the firm safer to match observed equity value and volatility, while in the second step, the higherξ$\xi$would move up the default boundary and increase the DM, canceling out its own effect in the first step.21Due to these two opposing forces, an increase inξ$\xi$has a small effect on the unified DM, as shown in Panel B of FigureIA.2.
  The limited net impact ofξ$\xi$on our empirical construction of the unified DM does not invalidate the presence of liquidity-driven default, as without our unified model, the relevant information contained in the equity market cannot be fully captured. This can be illustrated by the empirical performance of Merton's DM, which also uses equity market information to back out asset values and volatilities but fails to fully capture the increased liquidity-driven default in Phase III.22
  Focusing first on Phase III, when the SOE premium is the most severe, we see that adding unified DM as an explanatory variable reduces the SOE premium from a staggering 113 bps to a mere 6 bps. By contrast, adding Merton's DM increases the SOE premium slightly from 113 to 116 bps. These results indicate that, using our unified DM to explain cross-issuer credit quality, the severe divide in credit pricing between non-SOEs and SOEs disappears, while using Merton's DM for the same purpose exacerbates the divide. This contrast indicates the main mechanism of our model: because of the increased importance of government support during this period of heightened liquidity deterioration, the relative credit quality of SOEs as captured by our unified DM becomes significantly higher than that of non-SOEs. By contrast, “fundamental” credit quality as measured by Merton's DM does not capture this effect.
  Our unified DM works not only amidst the turmoil of Phase III, but also during the relative calm of Phase II. As shown in TableIV, adding the unified DM reduces the SOE premium in Phase II from 27 bps (t-statistic = 4.28) to 6 bps (t-statistic = 0.82). By contrast, adding Merton's DM increases the SOE premium from 27 to 32 bps (t-statistic = 5.05). Interestingly, adding government holdings directly as an explanatory variable can explain away the SOE premium in Phase III, and the regression coefficient on government holdings is small and insignificant during Phase II. These empirical results demonstrate the value of having a structural model and reaffirm the importance of our model mechanism. For the predefault period of Phase I, none of our explanatory variables works and the SOE premium remains at around 20 bps. This is a period when the credit market in China had not yet experienced any default, and hence, above and beyond credit ratings, credit quality does not seem to be important for credit pricing during this early period in China's credit market.
  Moving away from the predefined phases and illustrating the SOE premium results more continuously, Figure4plots the time series of the SOE premium at the quarterly frequency, after controlling for Merton's DM and our unified DM, respectively. Compared with the original SOE premium plotted in Figure1, we see that while Merton's DM cannot help resolve the SOE premium, the inclusion of our unified DM helps align the cross-sectional pricing in credit spreads and drives out the SOE premium.
  We incorporate a heterogeneous liquidity parameterξ$\xi$by calibrating to the average bond illiquidity (gamma) measured across non-SOE and SOE bonds by ratings in the three phases. Following the convention in China, we treat rating AA+ and above as high quality and AA and below as low quality. As a benchmark, we rescaleξ$\xi$to be close to one in Phase II to match the transaction cost.23We reestimate the unified DM and use it to explain the SOE premium, and find that the remaining unexplained SOE premium is 17 bps (t-statistic = 1.91), 6 bps (t-statistic = 0.75), and 17 bps (t-statistic = 1.22), respectively, in Phase I, II, and III.
  Overall, the added heterogeneity inξ$\xi$does not result in a significant improvement from our base-case results reported in TableIV. This result might be puzzling, as within the model, a higherξ$\xi$would result in a higher default probability. But this is true only if we keep asset volatility fixed. As discussed in SectionIII.B, by matching the model-implied equity value and equity volatility to the data via equation (13), our estimation strategy largely curtails the impact ofξ$\xi$on the empirical DM. It is therefore not surprising that allowingξ$\xi$to vary across firms does not improve the model performance. It also reinforces the observation that the market-observed equity volatility contains information not only about “fundamental” default risk but also about liquidity-driven default risk.24To the extent that there is heterogeneity in liquidity, the issuer-level equity value and equity volatility, which are the key inputs in our model estimation, can offer valuable information.
  To further illustrate the role of our model in extracting this valuable information from the equity market, we estimate the asset value and volatility directly from the firm's equity and bond volatilities without using our model, and then use our model to reestimate the unified DM and to explain the SOE premium.25Focusing on Phase III, we find that the remaining unexplained SOE premium is 88 bps (t-statistic = 4.97) without heterogeneity inξ$\xi$and 50 bps (t-statistic = 2.20) with the incorporation of heterogeneity inξ$\xi$as detailed in footnote . Two observations are worth noting. First, compared with our base-case results, this approach can only explain part of the SOE premium in Phase III, leaving a large component of the SOE premium (88 bps) unexplained. Second, in this setting,ξ$\xi$no longer influences the empirical estimation of asset volatility and becomes a free parameter. Consequently, allowing for heterogeneity inξ$\xi$improves performance, indicating that the information contained in the heterogeneousξ$\xi$is helpful in explaining the SOE premium. The fact that our base-case approach can explain a larger fraction of the SOE premium than the alternative approach suggests that by taking our unified model seriously, we can effectively extract liquidity information from equity volatility.
  We use the unified DM to study price discovery in China's credit market by focusing on the SOE and non-SOE samples separately. While the information content of credit spreads has been extensively studied for the U.S. market, our paper is the first comprehensive study of the Chinese market. From 2010 to 2020, this market is informed by two important shocks. The first-ever default in 2014Q1 changed investors' perception of credit risk and the 2018Q2 liquidity crisis underscored the importance of government support. Our empirical analysis aims to document their impact on price discovery.
  Before the first default in 2014Q1, credit spreads are uninformative with respect to credit quality after controlling for credit ratings. As shown in TableV, the sensitivity of credit spreads to Merton's DM is of a small magnitude for both the SOE and non-SOE samples and it has the wrong sign for the non-SOEs. Introducing unified DM does not have any additional impact. This lack of additional information content in credit spreads can be explained by the fact that bond investors had never experienced default before 2014Q1. Their perception of credit risk was such that credit ratings were a sufficient measure of credit risk.
  This perception was altered by the first-ever default. Moving from Phase I to II, the coefficients on Merton's DM become positive and significant: 1.59 (t-statistic = 2.70) for non-SOEs and 1.21 (t-statistic = 3.95) for SOEs. As credit-market investors became aware of the possibility of default risk, credit spreads started to incorporate default-related information above and beyond credit ratings. This includes information from the issuer's financial statements and equity pricing, key inputs to Merton's DM. Gauging the economic significance of this improvement in price discovery, we find that a one-standard-deviation increase in Merton's DM is associated with 24 and 23 bps increases in credit spreads, respectively, for non-SOEs and SOEs. During this period, the SOE premium is about 27 bps, and a one-notch increase in credit rating is associated with an average reduction in credit spread of 50 bps. From this perspective, the economic significance of the improved price discovery is sizable.
  When we introduce the unified DM, the extent of price discovery is similar to that of Merton's DM, indicating that the postdefault period of Phase II is dominated by information with respect to “fundamental” credit quality. Consistent with this view, government holdings as a direct explanatory variable is not important in explaining credit spreads during this period. Using all three variables together, however, we find that the unified DM remains important whereas Merton's DM does not. For the SOE sample, where the extent of government support is more important, the regression coefficient for Merton's DM declines to 0.47 (t-statistic = 1.54) while that for unified DM remains large and significant. This result indicates that even during normal liquidity conditions, the interaction between bailout probability and liquidity-driven default still matters in credit pricing.
  The liquidity crisis of 2018Q2 gives us a unique opportunity to study the impact of liquidity on price discovery. For the non-SOE sample, we find a marked improvement in the information content of credit spreads with respect to the “fundamental” credit quality captured by Merton's DM. As shown in TableV, the regression coefficient associated with Merton's DM increases from 1.59 (t-statistic = 2.70) to 7.53 (t-statistic = 3.67) for the non-SOE sample, and the corresponding incrementalR2$R^2$explained by Merton's DM increases from 0.9% to 2.8%. By contrast, the price discovery for the SOE sample does not exhibit any significant improvement using Merton's DM. The coefficient associated with Merton's DM increases from 1.21 (t-statistic = 3.95) in Phase II to 2.14 (t-statistic = 2.42) in Phase III, but the corresponding incrementalR2$R^2$explained by Merton's DM decreases slightly from 1.0% to 0.7%. Moreover, the economic significance of Merton's DM, as measured by the increase in credit spread associated with a one-standard-deviation increase in Merton's DM, is 79 bps for the non-SOE sample, a marked improvement from 24 bps in Phase II. By contrast, for the SOE sample, the economic significance decreases slightly from 23 to 20 bps.
  This contrast in price discovery reflects the key mechanism of our model. Without government support to alleviate the increased default under the worsening liquidity condition, credit spreads of non-SOEs become highly sensitive to credit quality (i.e., Merton's DM). By contrast, SOE issuers are not under the same severe concern over the fundamental credit risk. Under the overall liquidity deterioration, the content of their price discovery has instead become dominated by the information on government support. This increased importance of government support under worsening liquidity, while not captured by Merton's DM, is an important component of our unified DM. As a result, our unified DM can explain the improvement in price discovery for SOEs that is not captured by Merton's DM. As shown in TableV, moving from Phase II to III, the regression coefficient on our unified DM increases from 2.37 (t-statistic = 4.26) to 6.86 (t-statistic = 6.66) for the SOE sample, and the incrementalR2$R^2$explained increases from 1.8% to 4.6%. Moreover, the economic significance of our unified DM increases from around 23 bps in Phase II to 51 bps in Phase III, a stark contrast to the lack of improvement using Merton's DM for the SOE sample.
  Moreover, by incorporating all three components into a single measure, our unified DM can explain the price discovery for the non-SOE sample as well as SOEs during Phase III. The coefficient estimate associated with unified DM is 7.40 (t-statistic = 5.88), compared with 7.53 (t-statistic = 3.67) for Merton's DM. Comparing their economic significance, a one-standard-deviation increase in unified DM is associated with an increase in the credit spread of 105 bps, compared with 79 bps for Merton's DM. Moreover, the incrementalR2$R^2$explained by the unified DM is 4.0%, compared with 2.8% for Merton's DM. Using all three variables together, Merton's DM is no longer significant for the SOE sample, but remains significant for the non-SOEs in Phase III. We find that the additional information contained in Merton's DM is mostly related to the cross-issuer-level information on asset growth, which is a key input for Merton's DM but not for our unified DM.27
  To explain how price discovery in China's credit market evolves over time, we perform the panel regression in equation (16) over an eight-quarter rolling window. The regression coefficients for the DMs are plotted in Figure5. By reporting the price discovery continuously, this approach complements our results reported in TableV, where the panel regressions are performed for three phases.
  Over time, price discovery in China's credit market is transformed by two important shocks. The first-ever default in 2014Q1 changed investors' perception of credit risk. Prior to the shock, using either Merton's DM or our unified DM, the extent of price discovery, after controlling for credit ratings, is essentially zero. After 2014Q1, we see a visible improvement in the regression coefficient, reflecting investors' updated perception of credit risk after the first-ever default. Until the end of Phase II, the magnitude of price discovery moves very much in sync for SOEs and non-SOEs, indicating a shared concern over fundamental credit quality during this postdefault period.
  The shock introduced by the 2018Q2 credit tightening underscores the importance of government support amidst liquidity deterioration and exacerbates the divide between SOEs and non-SOEs in the credit market. In addition to the rapid divergence in credit spreads (i.e., the explosive SOE premium), we observe a divergence in the content of price discovery. Within the non-SOE sample, credit spreads become significantly more sensitive to the cross-issuer measures of “fundamental” credit quality. As shown in Figure5, the sensitivity of credit spreads on Merton's DM increases rapidly after the liquidity shock of 2018Q2, reflecting the impact of the increased likelihood of liquidity-driven default within this sample. For SOEs, where government support can help alleviate the increase in liquidity-driven default, there is no significant increase in price discovery along this dimension. Instead, credit spreads within the SOE sample become more sensitive to the extent of government support, reflecting the increased importance of government support under liquidity deterioration. Our unified DM, which integrates credit, liquidity, and government support into a single measure, can explain the credit spreads for SOEs as well as for non-SOEs, as shown in Figure5.

IV. On the Real Impact:   In this section, we study the real impact of the 2018 credit tightening by comparing the fundamental performance of SOEs and non-SOEs before and after the shock. Our main hypothesis builds on the observation that the rapid widening of the SOE premium after 2018Q2 reflects the worsening credit condition faced by non-SOEs in the broader economy, which subsequently impairs their fundamental performance relative to SOEs. While a complete understanding of the real impact is outside of our model, our attempts to shed light on this important topic, mostly empirical in nature, are as follows. In SectionIV.A, we document the rapid performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs after the 2018 credit tightening, in SectionIV.B, we perform event studies to establish a link between credit deterioration and performance deterioration for non-SOEs, and in SectionIV.C, we provide further discussion and consider alternative explanations.
  The post-2018Q2 explosion of the SOE premium reflects the deteriorating credit condition faced by non-SOEs relative to SOEs in China. Because of the fluidity of credit dispensation across the credit market, bank loans, and shadow banking, the financing difficulties experienced by non-SOEs exist not just in the credit market but rather are economy-wide. This motivates us to study the real impact of the credit tightening for a wider range of firms, not just those with active bond trading, which are used to study credit pricing in SectionIII. Expanding our sample to include firms with inactive bond trading, we have a sample of large and important nonfinancial firms in China with access to both the credit and equity markets. Summary statistics for this sample of issuers (821 non-SOEs and 623 SOEs) are reported in SectionIIof theInternet Appendix.28
  To directly compare the aggregate performance of non-SOEs against that of SOEs, Figure6plots the time series of the quarterly ROAs averaged across the non-SOE and SOE samples. Focusing first on non-SOEs, we see that with the exception of the downward trend from 2010 through 2012, the aggregate quarterly ROA of non-SOEs oscillates around a constant level of 1.20% until 2018 and is driven by strong seasonality, with first-quarter performance lagging significantly behind because of reduced economic activities during Chinese New Year celebrations. Against this pattern, the post-2018 performance of non-SOEs appears highly unusual. Within one quarter, the aggregate ROA crashes from 1.09% in 2018Q3 to −0.35% in 2018Q4, breaking the worst-ever record by a wide margin and driving the aggregate performance of non-SOEs deep into negative territory. Prior to this event, aggregate ROA varies within the narrow range of 0.87% to 2.14%. After its crash in 2018Q4, aggregate ROA bounces back during the first three quarters of 2019, only to plunge back to 0.02% in 2019Q4.
  Applying this regression for each quarter, Panel B of Figure6plots the quarterly performance gap, as measured bybNSOE$b^{{\it NSOE}}$in the regression above, between non-SOEs and SOEs. To smooth out the seasonality, we further plot the performance gapbNSOE$b^{{\it NSOE}}$using a rolling window of eight quarters and plot the smoothed version against the quarterly performance gap. Prior to 2018, the performance gap oscillates around an average level of 0.55% and remains significantly positive, consistent with the common perception that non-SOEs in China, though underprivileged, are more efficient than their SOE counterparts. Post-2018, this performance gap collapses precipitously and becomes indistinguishable from zero, driven mainly by the performance deterioration of non-SOEs, as shown in Panel A of Figure6.29
  Applying this regression to each of our three phases, Panel A of TableVIreports evidence on the performance gapbNSOE$b^{{\it NSOE}}$between non-SOEs and SOEs using both ROA and ROE. Before 2018Q2, the profitability gap is fairly stable andbNSOE$b^{{\it NSOE}}$is estimated at 0.56% and 0.53% in Phases I and II, respectively, which in annualized terms amounts to non-SOEs outperforming SOEs by around 2% per year. After 2018Q2, the ROA gap drops to 0.13% and becomes statistically insignificant. At the same time, the coefficient on equity size, a control variable, also increases sharply, indicating stronger performance deterioration for smaller firms, which are expected to be more vulnerable under credit tightening. We further conduct propensity score matching (PSM) to control for differences in other dimensions between non-SOEs and SOEs before the New Regulations.30We report the results using the matched sample in Panel B of TableVI. We again find a sharp decrease in the difference in ROA between non-SOEs and SOEs in Phase III. The same pattern is obtained using ROEs. Overall, under the severe credit condition, non-SOEs in China have on average lost their superior profitability relative to SOEs.
  We next use our government holdings measure to capture the extent of government support. As shown in TableVI, the results paint a similar picture as when the non-SOE dummy is used. Prior to 2018Q2, higher government holdings is in general associated with lower ROA and ROE, and, after 2018Q2, the negative relation weakens in magnitude and becomes statistically insignificant. Putting both the non-SOE dummy and government holdings in the same regression, we find that in Phases I and II, the non-SOE dummy is important in explaining the variation in ROA while the government holdings variable is unimportant. These results are consistent with our findings in credit pricing: before 2018Q2, government holdings is not important in explaining credit spreads above and beyond the NSOE dummy, while post-2018Q2, estimates for both variables become small and insignificant, reflecting the loss of non-SOEs' superior profitability relative to SOEs.
  Overall, our results document a post-2018 performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs. It should be emphasized that, with access to both credit and equity market financing, the firms included in our sample are among the largest in China. For smaller firms without access to the credit market, the impact could be even more severe.
  The results summarized in Panel B of TableVIIconfirm this hypothesis for the non-SOE sample and shed further light on the post-2018Q2 performance deterioration along the following four dimensions. First, consistent with the hypothesis that credit deterioration for non-SOEs leads to their subsequent performance deterioration, the regression coefficientsβDM$\beta ^{{\it DM}}$are negative and significant, both statistically and economically, for the non-SOE sample. Following a one-standard-deviation increase inΔDMi,t$\Delta {\it DM}_{i,t}$in 2018Q2, the underperformance averaged over the first two and eight quarters after 2018Q2 is 0.33% and 0.21%, respectively, comparable in magnitude to the diff-in-diff estimatorbNSOE$b^{\it NSOE}$that measures the gap in performance deterioration between non-SOEs and SOEs. Second, SOEs are less sensitive to deteriorating credit conditions. The regression coefficientsβDM$\beta ^{{\it DM}}$are negative for the SOE sample but statistically insignificant, indicating that the SOEs faced with more credit deterioration in 2018Q2 do not significantly underperform relative to their peers.33Third, using Merton's DM to capture the credit deterioration in 2018Q2, we do not find any predictability for either the non-SOE sample or the SOE sample, indicating that the additional information on liquidity and bailout incorporated into the unified DM is important in capturing the true extent of cross-sectional credit deterioration in 2018Q2. Lastly, to isolate the credit risk driven by liquidity and bailout beyond pure fundamentals, we create a new measure using the difference between our unifiedΔDMi,t$\Delta {\it DM}_{i,t}$and Merton'sΔDMi,t$\Delta {\it DM}_{i,t}$and repeat the predictability test. Not surprisingly, there is a negative relationship between credit deterioration in 2018Q2 and performance deterioration post-2018Q2 for the non-SOE sample but not for the SOE sample. This result confirms that credit deterioration is driven mainly by liquidity and government support beyond fundamentals.
  Building on the informativeness ofΔDMi,t$\Delta {\it DM}_{i,t}$in capturing firm-level credit deterioration, we sort all firms, SOEs and non-SOEs, in our sample by theirΔDMi,t$\Delta {\it DM}_{i,t}$att=$t=$2018Q2 into two groups. Firms in the high group have higherΔDMi,t$\Delta {\it DM}_{i,t}$in 2018Q2 and are faced with worsening credit conditions, while firms in the low group are less affected. Focusing on the channel of credit deterioration leading to performance deterioration, our hypothesis is that the relative performance deterioration between non-SOEs and SOEs is stronger for the more affected firms. To test this hypothesis, we perform the regression specified in equation (18) for the high and low groups separately. The results are summarized in Panel C of TableVII. First, in both the high and low groups, the diff-in-diff regression coefficientsbNSOE$b^{\it NSOE}$are negative and significant, reflecting the prevalence of the performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs. In particular, it is present not only for those firms more affected by the credit tightening (high group), but also for those less affected (low group). Second, comparing the magnitude ofbNSOE$b^{\it NSOE}$between the high and low groups, we find that, consistent with our hypothesis, the performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs is indeed stronger for firms facing more severe credit deterioration in 2018Q2. Specifically, estimated over the first two quarters after 2018Q2,bNSOE$b^{\it NSOE}$is−0.79%$-0.79\%$for the high group and−0.31%$-0.31\%$for the low group, compared with the full-sample estimate of−0.58%$-0.58\%$.
  Finally, using Merton'sΔDMi,t$\Delta {\it DM}_{i,t}$as the sorting variable in 2018Q2, we find the performance gapbNSOE$b^{\it NSOE}$between non-SOEs and SOEs to be negative and significant for both the high and low groups, but the differential effect between the two groups is not significant. Moreover, using the difference between our unifiedΔDMi,t$\Delta {\it DM}_{i,t}$and Merton'sΔDMi,t$\Delta {\it DM}_{i,t}$as the sorting variable in 2018Q2, the differential effect between the high and low groups becomes negative and significant. These results again confirm that firm-level information in the interaction between liquidity and bailout above and beyond fundamentals is important in capturing the credit deterioration experienced by firms in 2018Q2.
  Our model, built on exogenous firm dynamics, is designed for credit pricing. In considering the real impact, a hugely complicated but important issue, our model's role is limited to estimation of the credit condition faced by non-SOEs relative to SOEs. As to the exact channel through which the credit deterioration of non-SOEs can lead to performance deterioration, our model has little to say. In this section, we discuss this important issue and also address alternative explanations.
  Within the context of our model, having a bailout is always more efficient and improves credit pricing, as it helps to reduce bankruptcy costs by lowering the endogenous default boundary. Stepping outside of our model, the efficiency of bailing out SOEs is controversial and open to extensive debates. Similarly, within the context of our model, the performance deterioration of non-SOEs can be explained by the increasing financing costs faced by non-SOEs in the credit market. In reality, the real impact of the credit deterioration faced by non-SOEs does not stay merely at the level of financing costs, but can penetrate more deeply to affect firm fundamentals.34Faced with worsening credit conditions, non-SOEs might forgo new projects and stop injecting capitals into existing projects, while the less affected SOEs have the capacity to continue their operations and even take over projects from non-SOEs. To capture this deeper effect, a model that endogenizes the firm's production process is needed.
  Moving beyond economically focused models, the divide between non-SOEs and SOEs can extend past the realm of finance and economics. Central to this divide is the increasing importance of government support. Specifying and testing the channels through which government support can benefit SOEs over non-SOEs are beyond the scope of our paper. By focusing on the credit market, which we can directly observe and measure, our evidence is only one part, albeit an important part, of the societywide trend against non-SOEs. The explosion of the SOE premium captured from the credit market can very well reflect the market's expectation of this trend moving in favor of SOEs.
  Over the past several decades, non-SOEs in China, a tremendous engine of growth, have been competing for resources with more privileged and less efficient SOEs. Our empirical evidence points to an unprecedented reversal of that trend when non-SOEs begin to lose their superior performance relative to SOEs. Given the central importance of this topic for China's economy, these empirical facts warrant careful documentation and better understanding. Using firm-level credit shocks realized in the event quarter to predict their subsequent performance impacts, we find that the non-SOEs faced with stronger credit deterioration in 2018Q2 subsequently underperform more severely, while SOEs do not exhibit such cross-sectional sensitivity to credit conditions. This is our best attempt at establishing a link between credit deterioration and performance deterioration for non-SOEs. To explore the cross-sectional drivers of the post-2018Q2 performance deterioration of non-SOEs, we employ a number of explanatory variables, including firm-level leverage, rollover risk, and liquidity capacity in 2018Q2.35By far, our unified ΔDMevaluated in 2018Q2 is the best cross-sectional predictor of the performance deterioration of non-SOEs.
  As with any empirical tests, there are always potential alternative explanations that deserve further discussion and analysis. We consider two alternative hypotheses in particular. First, the U.S.-China trade war, which begins around the same time as the 2018 New Regulations, affects non-SOEs more severely than SOEs, leading to the performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs. Second, SOEs can better withstand the 2018 credit tightening because the government-mandated SOE deleveraging starting in 2016 helps strengthen their balance sheets relative to non-SOEs. Under these hypotheses, non-SOEs underperform relative to SOEs because of their own vulnerability that is unrelated to their lack of government support. As we show in robustness tests reported in TableVIII, the predictability of the sorting variables motivated by these alternative hypotheses is rather limited.
  In March 2018, the United States and China entered a contentious and still ongoing trade war. If the trade war affects non-SOE firms more severely than SOEs, then this event can potentially contribute to the performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs. We test this hypothesis by dividing our sample into groups of more- and less affected firms using three measures of trade war exposure.
  Our first measure is at the industry level. Over the course of the trade war, tariffs have been imposed on numerous industries.36Following the industry specification of Benguria et al. (2022), we sort firms by their respective industries into high and low groups, with the low group containing firms in industries that are not directly affected by the trade war. Our second and third measures are at the firm level. To obtain firm-level exposure, we further use information from the equity market. As the equity market can be driven by many factors, we focus on two short episodes when trade war news took center stage in the Chinese markets. The first episode occurred in June 2018, when the Chinese A-share market fell by over 8%, triggered by the tariff announcements and the anticipation of the eventual launch of the first wave of tariffs that began on July 6. We assume that firms with high beta during this month are viewed by investors as more affected by the trade war. The second episode occurred on May 6, 2019, the first trading day after China's Labor Day break, when the A-share market plummeted by 5.58%, triggered by President Trump's abrupt escalation of the trade war via Twitter. We assume that firms exhibiting more negative returns on this day have higher exposure.37
  We perform our robustness tests in Panel A of TableVIIIalong two dimensions. First, we examine the sensitivity of ΔROAi,t+τ$_{i,t+\tau }$to the trade-war-motivated sorting variables to see if they can explain the cross-sectional variation in changes in ROA within the SOE and non-SOE samples. The structure of this table follows that in Panel B of TableVII, except that the sorting variable in the current table is motivated not by credit exposure but by trade war exposure. Overall, we do not find strong evidence that the trade war is an important explanatory variable for the subsequent performance deterioration. Focusing on the results when the first-episode beta or the second-episode return are used, we find that during the first two quarters after 2018Q2, the sensitivity for SOEs is negative and significant, indicating that SOEs might be more sensitive to the trade war. Comparing the sensitivity coefficients between SOEs and non-SOEs, as reported under “NSOE − SOE,” we see that most of the coefficients are not significant or only weakly significant. More importantly, these trade-war-motivated sorting variables fail to explain the post-2018Q2 performance deterioration for non-SOEs.
  Second, we use the trade-war-motivated sorting variable to sort firms into high and low groups, and examine the relative performance deterioration between non-SOEs and SOEs for the two groups. The structure of this table follows that in Panel C of TableVII, except that the sorting variable in the current table is motivated not by credit exposure but by trade war exposure. If the trade war is indeed behind the performance deterioration of non-SOEs, we would expect the difference reported for the low group to be smaller, as it contains firms less exposed to the trade war. Up to the first four quarters after 2018Q2, we do not see strong results in support of this hypothesis. Extending the postevent window further to include 2019Q4, we see some evidence of this hypothesis. Specifically, using the one-day return on May 6, 2019 to capture trade war exposure, the difference in ΔROAi,t+τ$_{i,t+\tau }$between non-SOEs and SOEs is indeed smaller for the less affected firms in the low group over the window defined byτ∈[+1,+6]$\tau \in [+1,+6]$andτ∈[+1,+8]$\tau \in [+1,+8]$, that is, when averaged over the first six and eight quarters after 2018Q2. Among industries that are more affected by the trade war, the performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs is also marginally larger than less affected industries.
  Overall, to the extent that we can measure trade war exposure with accuracy, we find that it cannot fully explain the performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs. Our results can be further corroborated by the findings of Benguria et al. (2022). Using export and import data, they construct firm-level tariff exposures and find no significant difference between SOEs and non-SOEs. They further apply textual analyses on the annual reports of publicly listed firms to construct firm-level trade policy uncertainty. They find a trade-war-related decline in firm profits only later in 2019Q2 and Q3. Over the 2017Q4 to 2018Q4 horizon, they do not find any significant impact of the trade war on firm profits. This includes 2018Q4, when the postevent performance deterioration of non-SOEs is the most severe in our sample. Overall, these results indicate that the trade war is unlikely to be the main driver of the post-2018Q2 performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs.
  Under the deleveraging hypothesis, the 2016 to 2017 government-mandated SOE deleveraging cleans up the balance sheet of SOEs and makes them healthier than non-SOEs. As a result, SOEs are more resilient and can better withstand the credit tightening of 2018, while the overexpansion of non-SOEs during this period makes them vulnerable to the credit shock. Consistent with the timing and nature of the deleveraging campaign, average leverage, as measured by the ratio of book liabilities to book assets, has declined gradually from 57.4% in 2015Q4 to 55.7% in 2017Q4 for the SOEs in our sample, while for the non-SOEs in our sample, it has increased from 47.2% to 48.9%. Measuring leverage by book debts over book assets paints a similar picture. Also reflected in these numbers is the fact that SOEs are more levered than non-SOEs, even after the 2016 to 2017 deleveraging campaign.
  To test this deleveraging hypothesis, we compare the post-2018Q2 performance deterioration between the best non-SOEs against the worst SOEs. To capture the potential impact of the deleveraging campaign, especially for SOEs, we use three deleveraging-motivated sorting variables: (i) each firm's leverage just before the 2018 credit tightening, averaged over the four quarters before 2018Q2, (ii) each firm's change in leverage, calculated as the difference between the four-quarter average before 2018Q2 and the 2015 average, and (iii) each firm's change in leverage, calculated as the difference between the four-quarter averages before and after October 2016, when the SOE deleveraging was formally announced.38To the extent that the deleveraging campaign was effective, SOEs with larger reductions in leverage are those that improved under the hypothesis. Likewise, SOEs with lower levels of leverage right before 2018Q2 are more resilient under the deleveraging hypothesis.
  We test the deleveraging hypothesis in two steps. First, we examine the sensitivities of the post-2018Q2 performance deterioration to the deleveraging-motivated sorting variables. If the deleveraging campaign is an important driver of the post-2018Q2 performance deterioration, then we would expect the predictability coefficientsβX$\beta ^X$, which parallelβDM$\beta ^{{\it DM}}$, to be negative and significant, whereX$X$is one of the three deleveraging-motivated sorting variables, to reflect improved resilience contributing to lower performance deterioration for both non-SOEs and SOEs but especially for SOEs. The results are reported in Panel B of TableVIII. Using the level of leverage just before 2018Q2, the sensitivity coefficients are mostly positive and insignificant. When estimated over the first eight quarters after 2018Q2, the coefficients become positive and weakly significant among the SOE sample, indicating that SOEs with higher leverage do in fact deteriorate less. This is not surprising given that leverage is a highly endogenous variable and SOEs' leverage has been consistently higher than that of non-SOEs in part because of their government support. Using the change in leverage during the deleveraging campaign, we find some evidence that the SOEs that delevered more can subsequently withstand the credit tightening. In particular, the sensitivity coefficientsβX$\beta ^X$over the first two quarters after 2018Q2 are negative and significant for the SOE sample. For non-SOEs, most of the deleveraging-motivated sorting variables exhibit no predictability.
  Second, we compare the performance deterioration between non-SOEs and SOEs. To minimize the impact of the deleveraging campaign, we omit the more resilient SOEs from the benchmark group and focus on the SOEs that were less affected by the deleveraging campaign. To further sharpen the contrast, among the non-SOE sample, we omit the weaker non-SOEs to focus on the more resilient non-SOEs, using the same leverage measures. In effect, we compare the post-2018Q2 performance deterioration of the resilient non-SOEs to that of the less resilient SOEs, counter to the 2016 to 2017 trend of deleveraging SOEs and leveraging non-SOEs. As shown in Panel B of TableVIII, the post-2018Q2 performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs remains significant. Over the first two quarters after 2018Q2, the relative performance deterioration is 0.48%, 0.32%, and 0.26%, using the three deleveraging-motivated sorting variables. Compared with the full-sample result of 0.58%, the relative performance deterioration is less severe, indicating some explanatory ability of this deleveraging hypothesis. Given the inability of these deleveraging-motivated sorting variables to explain the post-2018Q2 performance deterioration for non-SOEs, however, the deleveraging hypothesis is unlikely to be the main driver of our results on the postevent performance deterioration of non-SOEs relative to SOEs.

V. Conclusions:   Motivated by the coexistence of SOEs and non-SOEs in China, we study the relative credit pricing between these two important segments of China's economy using a structural model of default that incorporates bailout probability in the model of He and Xiong (2012). Focusing on the divide of SOEs and non-SOEs in credit pricing, we quantify the extent of the financing disadvantage of non-SOEs relative to SOEs, which worsens dramatically amidst the government-led credit tightening. Under our structural default model that unifies credit, liquidity, and government bailout, this empirically estimated SOE premium captures the value of government support, which increases amidst liquidity deterioration as the presence of a government bailout helps alleviate the heightened liquidity-driven default. Moreover, SOEs and non-SOEs are divided not only in the level of bond pricing, but also in the content of their price discovery. The increased importance of government support makes SOEs more sensitive to bailout probability, while the heightened default risk increases non-SOEs' sensitivity to credit quality.
  Focusing on the divide of SOEs and non-SOEs in fundamental performance, we find that after 2018Q2, non-SOEs begin to lose their long-standing performance advantage over SOEs. Using our model-implied DM to capture the firm-level credit shocks realized in the event quarter, we find that the non-SOEs that face stronger credit deterioration in 2018Q2 subsequently underperform more severely, while SOEs do not exhibit such cross-sectional sensitivities to credit conditions. This is our best attempt at establishing a link between the credit deterioration of non-SOEs and their subsequent performance deterioration. Nevertheless, we find that the relative performance deterioration of non-SOEs over SOEs is present even among firms less affected by the credit deterioration, albeit with significantly smaller magnitudes than the more-affected firms. This result indicates that the disadvantage faced by non-SOEs goes beyond the credit channel. Specifying and testing additional channels through which government support can benefit SOEs over non-SOEs are beyond the scope of our paper.
  Competing with the more privileged SOEs for resource allocation, non-SOEs in China, an indispensable engine of growth, have managed to outperform SOEs for much of the recent economic development in China. From 2010 to 2018Q1, the non-SOEs in our sample consistently outperformed SOEs, with a quarterly ROA gap of 0.55%. Post-2018, however, driven mainly by the severe performance deterioration of non-SOEs, this performance gap collapsed precipitously, becoming indistinguishable from zero. As China moves on to the next phase of its development, how to maintain the coexistence of non-SOEs and SOEs is a hugely important topic. Our empirical findings call for government policies that better balance these two important segments of China's economy.
