TITLE:On the Magnification of Small Biases in Hiring
Abstract:  We analyze a setting in which a board must hire a chief executive officer (CEO) after exerting effort to learn about the quality of each candidate. Optimal effort is asymmetric, implying asymmetric likelihoods of each candidate being chosen. If the board has an infinitesimal bias in favor of one candidate, it allocates effort to maximize the likelihood of that candidate being chosen. Even when the board's prior is that its preferred candidate is inferior, she may still be chosen most often. A glass ceiling can also arise whereby the tendency to hire favored candidates increases as the importance of the position increases.
introduction:   Most organizations have a singleleader who must periodically be replaced. Boards choose new chief executive officers (CEOs), sports teams choose new head coaches, and CEOs choose new chief financial officers (CFOs) or divisional managers. These settings differ from those in which any subset ofN$N$options can be chosen because choosing one candidate inherently requiresnotchoosing others. Any effort to learn about one candidate is therefore either a complement or a substitute for effort to learn about others.
  In this paper, we show that the optimal effort that goes into choosing a new leader is often asymmetric—it is best to put varying levels of effort into learning about each candidate, even if candidates are ex ante identical. This causes the likelihoods of each candidate being selected to differ. If the board has an even infinitesimal bias in favor of one candidate, she is much more likely to be chosen. Moreover, a glass ceiling can arise in which biases are magnified most when the stakes are highest. These patterns result from profit-maximizing behavior, so competition will not solve the problem. Disparate hiring rates of those in favored and disfavored groups may therefore be large, especially at the executive level.
  We begin with a simple model in which a board must select a CEO from two candidates. Each candidate may be high or low quality and the board's prior for each is the same. It simultaneously chooses a pair of effort levels (“efforts”) that generate signals about each candidate's quality.1After observing these signals, the board forms posteriors and chooses a single candidate as the next CEO based on those posterior beliefs. We show that, perhaps surprisingly, the optimal levels of effort are asymmetric: the board arbitrarily chooses one candidate and invests substantial effort toward learning about her quality while devoting less effort toward learning about the other candidate. Because the assignment of efforts is arbitrary, there are two optimal effort allocations, each with the same pair of efforts but a different assignment of those efforts to candidates.
  Different effort allocations lead to different likelihoods of each candidate being chosen. While the board's expected payoff is identical for each of the two optimal effort allocations, the likelihoods of each particular candidate being chosen are not identical—from each candidate's perspective, the choice of effort matters.
  We next allow the board to have an infinitesimal bias in favor of one candidate. This bias eliminates the arbitrariness of the effort assignment—the assignment that maximizes the likelihood of the favored candidate being chosen is strictly optimal. As a result, the favored candidate is selected more than half of the time, often much more.
  One might expect that for important decisions like choosing a CEO, any infinitesimal bias would be overwhelmed by the effort that goes into making the right decision. We show that the magnification of the bias is higher when the importance of choosing a high-quality candidate is higher. The reason is that higher stakes mean across-the-board higher efforts and therefore more precise signals. When the signals are more precise, the likelihoods of good and bad news about each candidate are more unequal, and this increases the odds of the favored candidate being chosen. We call this result the “glass ceiling” effect.
  Suppose the board is biased in favor of candidateA$A$and signals are more likely to be positive than negative. In this case, the board invests more effort toward learning about candidateA$A$, usually receives more good news aboutA$A$’s quality than that ofB$B$, and choosesA$A$more often. If information is more likely to be bad than good, then the board invests more effort toward learning about candidateB$B$, usually receives more bad news aboutB$B$’s quality than that ofA$A$, and still choosesA$A$more often. The higher the stakes, the higher the effort and therefore the more likely that the preferred candidate is chosen.
  One might naturally be concerned that these results rest on knife-edge assumptions. Perhaps an infinitesimal bias can have a first-order effect on decision making when the board has identical priors concerning the candidates, for example, but what if the difference in priors for these candidates is larger than the bias? We show that it is irrelevant: even when priors are not identical, there are typically two solutions to the optimal effort problem that deliver identical expected payoffs to the board in the absence of a bias. Once a bias is present, the board will select the solution that features the highest likelihood that the preferred candidate is chosen.
  That efforts are asymmetric even when priors are ex ante identical is highly general. It applies no matter the number of candidates on the list, and it applies for many information structures. This core result does not follow simply from a toy model, but rather is an important property of many discrete-choice settings. We should therefore expect boards to exert unequal efforts toward learning about different candidates. Asymmetric efforts will result in turn in different likelihoods of each candidate being selected, with the candidate toward which the board is biased being favored.
  The reason for the generality of these results is that the restriction that at most one candidate can be chosen makes the payoff more convex in effort than the underlying information structure would suggest.2Suppose that substantial effort has been put into learning about candidateA$A$and less has been put into learning aboutB$B$. The marginal benefit of investing effort into learning aboutB$B$is low: if the information aboutA$A$is fairly positive—she seems like a good fit culturally, has relevant skills, experience, etc.—thenA$A$will be chosen most of the time. Even if the information aboutB$B$is also positive, it may not move the board's posterior as much because less effort was put into generating it. The board will be more confident about its positive view ofA$A$and will chooseA$A$. The same goes if information aboutA$A$is fairly negative: given how precise that information is,A$A$will notbe chosen even if the information aboutB$B$is negative as well. Since the information aboutB$B$is less precise, it is unlikely that this information will be pivotal in determining the board's choice between candidates. This makes learning aboutB$B$less valuable.
  Now consider the alternative case in which more is invested in learning aboutB$B$than in learning aboutA$A$. The marginal benefit toeven morelearning aboutB$B$is now high. SinceB$B$will tend to be chosen if the board receives good news and will not be chosen if the board receives bad news, then getting more precise news aboutB$B$’s quality is highly useful. Putting these two cases together, themarginal benefitto learning aboutB$B$is higher when thelevelof learning aboutB$B$is higher.
  This intuition has a close correspondence to real options. When the value of oil is low, the value of an oil well is low. An increase in the value of oil does not change the value of the well much because it is unlikely to be tapped either way. When the value of oil is high, further increases in that value substantially affect the value of the well because it is now likely to be tapped.
  By its nature, then, the problem of selecting a single leader will tend to favor unequal levels of effort, in which cases small biases can be greatly magnified. Sometimes the gap in efforts is small and sometimes it is large. The size of the gap depends on the information structure and the marginal cost of effort—but a gap is common.
  It is also often the case that glass ceilings arise, in which the magnification of bias increases as the importance of the decision rises. This result is not generally present but often is. A more important decision tends to increase the board's effort to learn about all candidates. If good news is more likely and higher effort tends to produce even more good news, or if bad news is more likely and higher effort tends to produce even more bad news, then a glass ceiling will arise. If the information structure is more complicated and the degree of good or bad news varies nonmonotonically with effort, then a glass ceiling may or may not arise.
  Our model is designed to match the setting of executive recruiting. How does this process work in practice? While there may be no precise process that is followed in all cases at all times, widely understood best practices have been extensively developed in fields beyond economics and finance.
  After the search firm presents the client with a short list of the top candidates, the client will conduct face to face interviews. During this stage, the client and search firm will discuss each candidate's resume, qualifications, personal strengths, and motivation before and after each interview. There is also an opportunity for the candidate to work with the search consultant to prepare for these interviews beforehand.
  By the time this step is reached, many of the more obviously unqualified candidates have been screened (Steps 6 to 8). Those who appear on the short list in Step 9 are likely to have similar prior quality distributions. The final evaluation of candidates on the short list is also simultaneous. These two features match our assumptions reasonably well.3
  Recently, concerns over inequality in executive hiring have been raised in the popular press.4As of 2021, only five CEOs of Fortune 500 firms were black and only 41 were female.5Of over 500 head coaches in the history of the National Football League (NFL), only 24 have been black.6While it is difficult to prove that these facts are the result of biases, former head coach Brian Flores made this claim in a 2022 lawsuit against the NFL and its teams. Our model provides policy recommendations for solving this problem, to the extent that it exists.
  First, simply reducing the size of board members' biases will not necessarily have much effect on the frequencies with which favored candidates are chosen. If policy makers want to reduce the gap in these frequencies, then educating decision-makers about implicit bias may not be very useful. This is consistent with substantial research surveyed in Kalev, Dobbin, and Kelly (2006) that suggests implicit bias training does not work. Second, a rule like the NFL's Rooney Rule, which requires interviews of at least one minority candidate for any head coaching position, is also unlikely to solve the problem. The issue arisesaftera short list has been created.
  Third, a rigorously enforced rule that efforts to learn about a candidate must be equal across candidates could solve the magnification of bias problem. Interestingly, employment attorneys typically advise clients to employ equal levels of effort in learning about candidates in interviews to avoid liability for appearing biased in the hiring process. While precisely equal efforts cannot feasibly be enforced, mitigating those differences can mitigate the importance of bias in the model. Furthermore, the nonprofit Society for Human Resource Management, citing substantial academic and nonacademic literature, argues that structured interviews are more effective for executive recruitment than unstructured interviews (e.g., Zaccaro (2010)). If structured interviews are interpreted as “identical efforts,” then they are a possible solution to the problem identified in this manuscript.
  The paper is organized as follows. After reviewing the literature in SectionI, in SectionIIwe present our baseline model with two candidates, two types, and two possible signals. We show that (i) efforts to learn about each candidate always differ, and thus (ii) the likelihoods that each candidate is chosen differ. As a result, (iii) if the board is biased in favor of a candidate, that candidate will be chosen more often. In SectionIII, we develop a similar model with a continuum of signals. The same results hold. In SectionIV, we show that for both models, (iv) a glass ceiling arises whereby the gap in effort levels grows as the board's choice increases in importance, leading to divergent outcomes. The results are nearly identical in these two models, and each helps clarify why optimal efforts are asymmetric.
  In SectionV, we allow the preferred candidate to be believed to be lower quality ex ante. In theInternet Appendix, we allow for many candidates, many types, and arbitrarily complex countable signal structures.7Throughout, (i) to (iii) continue to hold. In SectionVI, we investigate the generality of the glass ceiling result (iv) and show that it may or may not hold depending on the information structure.
  In SectionVII, we either restrict the board so that it learns exogenously, or allow the board to choose its information structure flexibly, such that the distribution of signals conditional on a candidate's type need not depend only upon effort. When the board cannot undertake effort, small biases are typically not important, as they are unlikely to overcome any difference in priors that the board may have for its short-list candidates. Bias is important in our model because it affects the choice of efforts. Interestingly, we find that when information can be flexibly designed, the likelihoods of each candidate being chosen are the same, so small biases are mitigated rather than magnified. That is, there is a nonmonotonicity in the effect of information on bias: if the board is unable to invest in information or can flexibly design its information structure, bias is mitigated. If the board can learn but is unable to choose its information structure, bias is magnified.
  We conclude in SectionVIIIby offering recommendations as to how to take the model to the data and by discussing policy implications. The latter include modifications to the hiring process that can reduce the magnitude of the documented effect, as well as modifications that are likely to fail. Our results also have implications for career and schooling choices for members of favored and disfavored groups who expect this treatment in the future. We provide additional results in theInternet Appendix, such as predictions for the average quality of accepted and rejected candidates in our model, as compared to a standard model in which there is a lower bar for favored candidates.

I. Literature:   The literature that relates to this manuscript is extensive. For the sake of brevity, we point to classic papers in the field as well as to surveys.
  Our setting is one of discrete choice under uncertainty. Weibull, Mattsson, and Voorneveld (2007) study, as we do here, a model in which a decision-maker receives imperfect signals about the value of various options prior to selecting a single item from this set. Within this context, the authors give conditions under which the decision-maker optimally selects the item with the highest signal and under which the decision-maker's expected utility is increasing in the items' payoffs. We build on their work by characterizing the amount of information the decision-maker chooses to acquire about each item and by studying the effects of small biases on the likelihood each item is selected. Matějka and McKay (2015) study a similar choice model applying the rational inattention approach to information frictions. They show that under an optimal information strategy, the probability the decision-maker chooses a particular item conditional on the state of nature is in line with a generalized multinomial logit model. In SectionVII, we apply their solution to our baseline model and show that when the board can flexibly determine the types of information to acquire, small biases are ameliorated rather than magnified through endogenous learning.
  The choice setting we study is also closely related to the so-calledbanditliterature, in which an actor can experiment with different slot machines ad infinitum (Rothschild (1974), was seminal; see Bergemann and Valimaki (2008) for a review). The actor is never obligated to choose a single machine, but typically one machine will pay off often enough that she will stick with that machine forever. In some sense, bandit problems are sequential learning versions of our model, which features simultaneous learning. If the payoff distributions for each option are ex ante identical, then the likelihood that the actor chooses a particular option is not constant across options. Whatever arms the actor pulls initially lead to path dependence in her eventual choice.
  For the applications that we discuss in the introduction, learning is most likely some combination of simultaneous and sequential learning. One does not usually choose a CEO after a five-year trial run, nor does one choose a CEO after one round of interviews. That said, the similarity of some of our models' implications with those from the bandit literature suggests that our results are robust.
  Beyond the literature on discrete choices, there is a theoretical literature concerning the interaction between small differences in initial beliefs, information, or bias and the effect of learning on those differences. In the same vein as our manuscript, for example, Van Nieuwerburgh and Veldkamp (2009) find that small informational differences concerning asset values are amplified by learning: it is profitable to focus effort on acquiring information that others do not have. Van Nieuwerburgh and Veldkamp (2009) apply this intuition to explaining home bias in portfolio choice, and Van Nieuwerburgh and Veldkamp (2010) extend the concept to underdiversification more broadly.
  A considerable literature analyzes the optimal design of tournaments in which candidates exert effort and prizes are awarded based on relative outcomes. These models are similar to ours in that a decision-maker chooses a single candidate from a set of options, but differ in that the decision-maker designs the rules of the tournament while candidates exert effort to sway the decision-maker.
  In these models, when players are asymmetric it is often optimal to design a biased contest in which one side must outperform the other by a sufficient margin to claim the prize (see, e.g., Lazear and Rosen (1981), Meyer (1991), Tsoulouhas, Knoeber, and Agrawal (2007), Ederer (2010), Kirkegaard (2012), Franke et al. (2013), Kawamura and de Barreda (2014)). This is the case whether the goal is to maximize effort or to select the highest-quality candidate (as in our model). It has historically been believed that contests should be symmetric when players are homogeneous ex ante, but, in fact, contests can be optimally biased even when contestants are ex ante identical (Drugov and Ryvkin (2017)).
  Our model shares some similarities to this literature, but also has some important differences. If we assume that the decision-maker's goal is to choose the highest quality candidate, consider the decision-maker's choice of efforts to be the “design of the contest,” and restrict the candidates to taking no actions, then the asymmetric optimal effort expenditures in our model are akin to the idea that the contest is optimally biased. In the standard tournament literature, however, the design is not costly for the designer and contestants can take actions.
  A theoretical literature has also considered the presence of biased beliefs. If learning the truth is important, how and why do biased beliefs persist? Bordalo et al. (2016) model stereotypes as arising from an overweighting of representative types. If two populations are in general similar but differ in one or two areas, people tend to identify those populations with those areas (e.g., Floridians are elderly). In similar work, Gennaioli and Shleifer (2010) model beliefs that are biased by limited recall and allow for learning. Their model's results are consistent with several judgment biases as well as portfolio anomalies.
  Bartos et al. (2016) run field experiments in which decision-makers can exert effort in reading applications and complement this analysis with a simple model in which a decision-maker can apply costly prescreening to candidates. If the conditional likelihood of passing a prescreen is higher for preferred groups, then such a screen magnifies any bias that the decision-maker has a priori by skewing the pool of final applicants in favor of preferred groups. Importantly, whether a bias impacts the screening decision depends in part on the size of the bias, so this model suggests that small biases should have only a limited effect on selection outcomes. Our model differs in that even infinitesimal biases are greatly magnified through the decision-maker's information choice problem.8
  A substantial literature in psychology documents the prevalence of conscious and subconscious biases in many areas. Biases may be harmful for society but arise because they are helpful for certain individuals or “tribes.” For example, summarizing decades of work, Kahneman (2011) argues that biases are useful for quick thinking. Consider the asymmetric cost in estimating the likelihood that a large, dark shape moving behind a bush is due to a dangerous animal. If I believe that it is likely to be dangerous then I may waste energy, but if I believe that it is not likely to be dangerous I may be dead. Kahneman (2011) provides an extensive review of the literature on biases.
  Prior literature also provides extensive evidence on gender gaps in occupations and pay. The causes and size of the pay gap appear to result from experience on the job, hours worked, occupational choice, negotiating approach, employer bias, health insurance costs, among other factors. Still, some of the pay gap remains unexplained, and the literature has not determined the relative importance of explanatory factors, in part because many interact. An example of particular relevance for our paper is occupational choice: the fact that the fraction of women differs by occupation is likely to be due at least in part to bias by employers, in which case attributing gaps to occupational choice rather than bias is misleading. Bertrand (2011) and Blau and Kahn (2017) provide partial summaries of this literature.

IV. The Glass Ceiling:   It would not be surprising if small biases matter when decisions are unimportant, but a board's choice of CEO is of first-order importance. We therefore investigate whether the magnification of bias is larger when getting the choice right is more important. This question is relatively easy to answer within the confines of our two stylized models.
  Corollary 3. (The Glass Ceiling)In both the2×2×2$2\times 2\times 2$model and the2×2×∞$2\times 2\times \infty$model, if the priorQ≠1/2$Q\ne 1/2$, then the likelihood that the board chooses candidateA$A$is strictly increasing in the importance of the decision,V$V$.

Moreover, the information structure is such that, in situations in which the board allocates effort to its preferred candidateA$A$, greater effort implies a higher likelihood of good information, and in situations in which the board allocates effort to candidateB$B$, greater effort implies a higher likelihood of bad information. These effects imply that the difference in selection likelihoods for the two candidates is larger when the decision is more important. We analyze the generality of this result in SectionsVandVIand show that a glass ceiling often does, but need not, arise.

The same is true if bad news is more likely and higher effort tends to produce even more bad news. If the information structure is more complicated, perhaps differing from one candidate to the next (as in the preceding example), or if the degree of good or bad news varies nonmonotonically with effort, then a glass ceiling may or may not arise.

VII. Flexibility of Learning:   We argue thatendogenouslearning on the part of the board exacerbates the likelihood that the board selects its preferred candidate in the presence of an infinitesimal bias. One weakness of this argument is that, when candidates are ex ante identical, small biases could have large effects on selection outcomes even when learning is exogenous. The reason is that, with two signals and identical priors, there is a high probability that the board's posterior beliefs regarding the quality of the two candidates are the same after observing the realized signals. A small bias breaks these ties in favor of the preferred candidate, so the preferred candidate is chosen most often. For example, suppose information is determined exogenously and the board's signals for candidatesA$A$andB$B$are equally precise.   The stark effect of an infinitesimal bias with exogenous learning is knife-edge. When candidates are not ex ante identical, exogenous information does not inherently favor candidate A. To see this, suppose that information is determined exogenously and let the board's signals for candidatesA$A$andB$B$be equally precise. In the limit as the bias vanishes, each candidate is selected half of the time.
  When information is flexible and in the limit asb→0$b\rightarrow 0$, the board's optimal information strategy leads each candidate to be chosen equally often. This means that with flexible learning, small biases are ameliorated rather than magnified through the board's optimal choice of information. The magnification of a bias therefore relies crucially on the assumption that the structure of information is somewhat fixed—that is, the board is limited in the types of investigations that are feasible—rather than determined flexibly by the board.
  From a policy perspective, this idea runs counter to what is typically suggested by executive recruitment specialists who argue for inflexible, structured interviews in which all candidates are asked identical questions. While the analysis of exogenous learning in previous sections suggests that limiting the board's flexibility in running investigations—by requiring equal efforts toward learning about each candidate—is one possible solution to the problems raised in this paper, the results in this section show that flexibility can be a double-edged sword.

VIII. Conclusion:   We analyze the optimal behavior of a board that must choose a CEO from a pair of ex ante identical candidates. The board can exert effort to learn about the quality of each candidate before making its choice. It may have a bias in favor of one of the candidates, but the bias is small relative to the importance of choosing the best candidate. This very simple problem results in four surprising findings.
  First, the board's optimal effort choice is asymmetric: if it is unbiased, then it exerts more effort learning about one candidate than the other.
  Second, asymmetric effort means that, once it has decided upon the allocation of efforts to candidates, the likelihoods of choosing each candidate are not equal.
  Third, if the board has a small bias in favor of one of the candidates, this removes the arbitrariness of the allocation of efforts to candidates: it will choose an assignment that maximizes the likelihood of its favored candidate being chosen. The favored candidate is more likely to be chosen even if the bias is infinitesimal and the favored candidate is less likely to be good, ex ante.
  These three results, which are the most important to come from the model, are highly general. They result from most information structures, including effectively all structures that yield a discrete posterior distribution and some that yield continuous posterior distributions.
  Fourth, there exist information structures for which a glass ceiling arises: the more important the decision, the larger the magnification of small biases. This is perhaps surprising, as one might expect biases to be mitigated by high stakes and by the opportunity to learn about candidate quality. We show that, at least in our model, learning and stakes do not necessarily mitigate the problem. In fact, stakes can exacerbate it.
  Becker (1971) argues that market forces should mitigate discrimination in hiring. In our setting, this is not necessarily the case (though in other settings, of course, it is). In the limit as the bias vanishes, the board is maximizing profit, yet differences in selection outcomes persist. Even in SectionVwhen the favored candidate is less likely to be high quality, the board does not err in choosing a solution favoring her. Inequality is not equivalent to inefficiency so, as a result, competition does not drive out inequality.
  We note that we do not directly model competition among employers for the specific set of candidates under consideration, that is, the board is free to choose any candidate that it wishes. There are two reasons for this modeling choice. First, a strategic game involving multiple employers inferring others' information from each other's actions is beyond the scope of the paper. Modeling this would require substantial simplifications elsewhere that would limit the generality of our results. Second, many or most executive searches are not highly competitive in this way. Cziraki and Jenter (2021) find that 80% of CEO hires at large, public U.S. firms are insiders and a further 10% are previously personally connected to the board. It is unlikely in most cases of an active search that most candidates are also candidates in other searches. While economists often take high pay to be indicative of competitive markets, it may simply mean that there is high demand for talent, rather than intense competition for talent at any particular time. Furthermore, Edmans, Gosling, and Jenter (2021) find that high CEO pay may be better understood to reflect fairness concerns of CEOs and boards than “market rates.”
  If competition cannot solve the problem, what policies could? A policy requiring the board to consider more candidates from disfavored groups, like the NFL's Rooney Rule, is not promising. Increasing the number of disfavored candidates in a hiring pool need not increase the likelihood that the board chooses a disfavored candidate. Implicit bias training will also be ineffective, as the size of the bias is not the problem. Any bias, no matter how small, has this effect.
  The policy that most directly addresses the issue is one mandating equal efforts toward learning about each candidate. To the extent that effort, like time spent interviewing a candidate, is measurable, this policy could be effective, but it is unclear whether in practice this policy could be enforced. Zaccaro (2010) cites substantial academic and practitioner research suggesting that structured interviews are optimal. The termstructuredmeans predetermined questions that are designed to provide precise comparisons between candidates. This can be taken to indicate a means to ensure equal efforts toward learning about each candidate. In light of our results on flexible information design, it may be the case that well-designed, flexibly determined, nonidentical interview questions may also solve the problem of bias in hiring.
  We conclude by noting that if biases are correlated positively across firms—that is, if firms tend to be biased in favor of the same sorts of candidates—then people would likely change their behavior prior to reaching candidacy. For example, some individuals might work harder to overcome biases, though we see in SectionVthat this could be futile. Others might underinvest in new skills if acquiring those skills is costly and if firms' biases are likely to prevent these individuals from being hired in the future. In either case, inequity in the hiring process, which is the focus of this paper, may spill over into inefficiency in workers' decisions to invest in themselves. The effect of future evaluative biases on skill development has been studied in past work. Phelps (1972), for example, shows how beliefs that some workers tend to have low skill causes managers to assign them to unskilled positions. This makes the acquisition of skill for these workers unattractive and the prophesy self-fulfilling. A subsequent literature further develops this idea, perhaps most notably Cornell and Welch (1996), Athey, Avery, and Zemsky. (2000), and Bernhardt, Hughson, and Kotsoati (2006).
